In article <slrnehqe5o.3rj.bruno@wolff.to>, Bruno Wolff III wrote:

On 2006-09-29, David E. Cohen <david_e_cohen@yahoo.com> wrote:

No, simultaneous movement isn't "perfect information".  But it is far 
superior to randomness.


If the random events have a finite number of possible outcomes with rational
probabilities (which is normally the case), and the game is zero sum, then
there is an equivalent game without (expilicit) randoness that uses
simultaneous moves to generate the same effects.


Untrue, because there is an element of psychology in simultaneous move games
that is absent in random mechanisms.  By your hypothesis, rock-paper-scissors
is equivalent to throwing dice to see who will be high-roller, and it isn't.
The human mind is very bad at generating true randomness; you produce patterns
which the opponent can detect and exploit.  Expert players of rock-paper-
scissors (yes, there are such) will detect your patterns and beat you fairly 
consistently.
