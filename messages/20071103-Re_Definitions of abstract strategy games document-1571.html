I disagree with this.  I've only heard of Backgammon being excluded as
an abstract game because of its random element of dice.  I've never
heard of it being included as an abstract game.

The name of the organization is IAGO and should therefore concern
itself with abstract games.  That would indicate following commonly
accepted definitions and would preclude inventing your own definitions
such as "an abstract game can include random elements or hidden
information but not both".  I *never* heard that before you made it
up.

The two primary, completely objective criteria for determining what an
abstract game is are:
1) No random elements.
2) No hidden information.

In what's proving to be a difficult search for objectivity, these two
criteria are like diamonds.  When you take the liberty of combining
these two criteria in a mutually exclusive manner, you smash these two
diamonds with a hammer.

Why don't we preserve the integrity of the two diamonds and exclude
all games with random elements and all games with hidden information.
So we lose Backgammon.  So what?  Who gives a shit?  Everyone I've
heard of doesn't consider it to be an abstract game anyway.  So we
lose Kriegspiel.  Again, who really gives a shit?  We still have
hundreds of abstract games to deal with besides Backgammon and
Kriegspiel.

Here's the beauty of keeping these two objective criteria intact:
They knock out all designer games, all war games, all german games,
Carcasonne, Puerto Rico, Night of Dracula, and every game that has a
stack of shuffled action cards or character cards or whatever kinds of
cards there are.  Do you see the beauty in that, Rich?  Now we have a
*completely objective* means of excluding thousands of games that
don't belong.
