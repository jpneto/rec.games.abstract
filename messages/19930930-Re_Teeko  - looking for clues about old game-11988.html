Basically, in the "standard opening" you simply place your tokens one at a
time, as you might expect.  In the other 15 variants, you sometimes get to
choose your *opponent's* play instead of your own.  (The standard opening
corresponds to the empty set.)


You didn't say anything about what "pointing" to a cell could mean, or what
the opponent playing one of your tokens could be.


Pointing to a cell is the proper etiquette for indicating where your opponent
is required to play.  From a game-theoretic viewpoint, it would be equivalent
to simply picking up one of your opponent's tokens and placing it on the
board; but if you do it that way you're likely to get confused about whose
turn it is.  (Because it's the token-color that alternates for the first 8
moves, *not* the identity of the player making the decision.)
