I suspect you are misinterpreting my statement because you are
saying something similar.  Let me try to clarify myself.  Suppose
you are playing a game (chess, go, whatever) against a computer
and further suppose you are not aware of any instances of a
computer program using strategy X.  Now suppose that much to your
surprise, the computer program uses strategy X.  Your reaction
might be

"Whoa!  I didn't know a computer program could do THAT!"

or equivalently,

"What?!  Computer programs aren't supposed to be able to do THAT!"

Such a statement in no way implies "can't" or "impossible."
Quite the opposite.  You would conclude that the surprising instance
of a computer program using strategy X proves that it is possible,
if it weren't for the fact that I specifically pointed out that
the computer program didn't really use strategy X (sacrifice material
for strategic gains) but instead used strategy Y (sacrifice material
for tactical reasons) to choose a move that superificially appeared
to arise from strategy X.
