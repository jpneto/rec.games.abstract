Thanks for posting this, Mark. You surprised me! And thanks for the questions. They will help me clarify something I did not realize needed clarification, and I appreciate that. As for online play, the late Larry Lynn Smith put a version in zillions: http://www.zillions-of-games.com/cgi-bin/zilligames/submissions.cgi?do=show;id=1033 This version does not necessarily allow all the current rules, as it was made some years ago. Abdul-Rahman Sibahi made a 2-player version for play by email here: http://play.chessvariants.org/pbm/play.php?game%3DJoe+Joyce+Hyperchess%26settings%3DJoe+Joyce+Hyperchess+ I, sadly, am a dinosaur when it comes to modern computer/internet tech, so I may not be able to give more diagrams for the game. And not all the links on the various pages are active any more, nor can I fix them. :( For face-to-face play and to follow the example game given, there is a PDF for a printable board here: http://www.chessvariants.org/3d.dir/hyperchess_board.pdf It is adequate.

The game, starting with the board, came from my problems with linear differential equations in college. I was unable to visualize 4 spatial dimensions, so I drew a small piece of 4D graph paper to help me understand 4D. It didn't help with the math, but it occurred to me that it would make a very interesting chess board. The board is a double grid piece of graph paper, 16x16 squares, representing 16 4x4 2D "tiles" laid out in a 4x4 pattern. This is the numbering system for both the "small squares" and the "big squares": 
                                                                                           | 41 | 42 | 43 | 44 |
                                                                                           | 31 | 32 | 33 | 34 |
                                                                                           | 21 | 22 | 23 | 24 |
                                
           | 11 | 12 | 13 | 14 |

Each square of the board has a 4 digit position number, the first two numbers being the "big square", and the second two numbers being the "small square". So there are actually 16 small squares with the same 2-digit position number, 1 in each of the 16 "big squares". As in standard chess, only 1 piece/pawn may occupy any one "small" square. As a piece moves from one big square to the next, it always lands on the "same" (aka; corresponding) "little square" in the big square moved into. I hope that makes sense. I will try to talk someone into doing some diagrams for this, but...
