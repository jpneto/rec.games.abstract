Rich Hutnik wrote:
In short, a player plays two games at same time (one of each color),
until they lose a game, and then they are eliminated. Players play until two
players are left, and then the player with most wins, wins the
tournament.

Players tying can be handled one of several ways. One way is to have
players replay the tie until there is a winner, or one of two players
is eliminated by an opponent. The other way to handle a tie is to have
the tie eliminate both players.

Interesting. A few things strike me.

One is that it would be possible for one individual to play many games and have none of them come to conclusion because their opponents keep losing in their other simultaneous match. I would imagine it might be frustrating to have your game at an interesting point, and then it is called off and a new one set up with a new opponent.

When down to the final three, it seems very open to manipulation. If two players agree to stall on their common board (making quick non-threatening moves to save time and brain power), they can likely force the third player out on grounds of time or by direct win.

The way of handling ties is likely to be critical, especially as in chess they seem relatively common. Letting the tie stand is not much good as it again seems to permit alliances, where two players exchange down to a tie, so as to concentrate on eliminating their other opponent.

It is basically a different 'game' from standard FIDE chess just through having to play two sides at once. This may make rule changes like "tie = elimination" more acceptable, but I can't see regular chess tournaments jumping to use this format.
