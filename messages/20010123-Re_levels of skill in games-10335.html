In article <dh6n1ciarb1.fsf@pc-gtw.research.att.com>, Gary Wong 
<gtw@research.att.com> wrote:


We are supposed to consider that the games are
played under reasonable conditions (e.g. the backgammon statistic
shows the result for a relatively long match that might be found in
a tournament, while the go result might be for a single game, to
account for the fact that a single backgammon game is much shorter
than a game of go).


But this freedom allows you to make your favorite game seem to have as many 
skill levels as you want -- just play longer matches to exaggerate the 
probability that the higher skilled payer wins.

For instance, the number of "skill levels" in chess match play (say of an 
eight-game match) should be much higher than the number of skill levels in 
chess single-game play (which is what the usual Elo rating measures).
Does this mean chess matches are a much deeper game, requiring all sorts of 
additional knowledge that one couldn't gain from single games?  I don't 
think so.
