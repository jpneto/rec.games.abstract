I had the same objection about themes being allowed.  In my mind, not
that I'm an authority on abstract games, but in my mind abstract games
are best defined by what they don't have: no random elements, no
hidden information (which would exclude simultaneous move games), and
(I always thought) no theme.

Now I've learned that there is some degree of consensus that abstract
games may be "lightly themed."  People who play lightly themed games
will tell you that the light theme has absolutely no bearing on the
gameplay and that such games are "essentially" abstract.  Still, I
have some difficulty letting go of my view of abstract games being
themeless, Reversi, Go, and Checkers being good examples.

I'm broadly in agreement with this, and would like to expand on it. From
what I've read on this topic over the past few weeks I have the
impression of an underlying assumption that games either are or are not
abstract, as if abstraction and its opposite (representation?) are
watertight compartments. I think it is more accurate to regard games as
falling somewhere on a linear continuum from pure abstraction at one
end (Go, Draughts/Checkers, Othello etc) to what might be called
extreme representationalism at the other - that's the end where role-
playing games begin to merge into an overlap between game-"play" and
dramatic or theatrical "plays". Perhaps half way between the two
extremes come games like Hare & Tortoise (if you'll pardon the
presumption), which is essentially abstract but dressed up in a
picturesque thematic disguise to sweeten the underlying pill of complex
arithmetical calculations (if you'll pardon the mixed metaphor). A bit
further to the representational end comes Monopoly, in which you are
actually invited to play the part of a landlord and the skills involved in
play are a fair representation of the relevant real-life skills.

It doesn't follow, however, that any given game can necessarily be placed
at an exact point on the scale between one end and the other of the
abstract/representational continuum. Much may depend upon the way it
is perceived by the players, even by different players at the same table.
Some may be more conscious of the thematic referent than others, really
imagining themselves to be playing a thematic role, while others may
ignore it entirely and turn all its eventualities into a mental abstraction.

There also appear to be several other underlying assumptions as to what constitutes an abstract game. Three in particular are (1) that it is a game of perfect information - that is, no part of the current position is hidden (which for example cuts out Kriegspiel); (2) that it is a game of pure strategy - that is, progress is not influenced by randomising elements such as dice (which for example cuts out Backgammon); and (3) the starting position should be invariable (which for example cuts out Chess960 and nearly all card games).

I don't think any of these features should, in general, be regarded as essential to the definition of "abstract", which for my money simply means at least non-representational and ideally non-referential. (Reversi
and Othello are the same game, and both non-representational, but
Othello by its very title is referential, giving literary significance to the conflict between black and white.) However, that's no reason why
IAGO should not simply say that it exists to promote those abstract
games which exclude randomising elements, hidden information, and
variable starting positions. Or, to express it more positively, which are
games of pure strategy, perfect information, and invariable starting
positions.
