I'm not sure this is intended to be humorous or not, but it did make me chuckle :)

I'm fairly sure I didn't make any claim that Chess was good, just that it was popular, which is a statement of fact. It felt implied in the post that I was responding to that popularity in some way correlates with quality - the purpose of my post was rather to highlight that that isn't a logical conclusion.

Likewise, balance, draw rate and depth aren't necessarily objective either, as at the end of the day, each comes down to a subjective priority judgement of their importance. I personally feel that balance is essential, and my objective criteria for that would be that the game isn't fun if two experts always know that one colour will win. Draw rate and depth are harder to argue. It's fairly easy to subjectively argue that in a perfect game, perfect play equates to a draw. This doesn't in itself lead to a high draw rate, but it does mean that a draw would need to be a viable conclusion to a game.. Go is a game where I actually feel that a draw should be viable, so I've never liked fractional komi.

Depth is not only hard to argue, but also hard to quantify. Is depth best measured in move-tree depth, breath, or total nodes (forgive me for using AI terminology, it's the one I'm most familiar with). How do you quantify depth where the nature of the evaluation itself changes (such as redstone, where the red stones in affect distort the playing board itself)?

Nick made a post that I thought was particularly good regarding the importance of accessibility. Again, that's a subjective judgement, but if I was to put some of my design ideas on paper to share, I'd like to think people would play it - the more accessible it is, the more likely I am to get participants.

Also, there's the likeability factor. If no-one in the world had ever played an abstract board game, and suddenly played a single game of each one ever written, which would feel most compelling, and why? Does "best game" come down to one with theoretical depth and balance, or one that both beginners and experts alike can play competitively with a fair chance of winning and find fundamentally fun?

Just because the design of games requires a lot of attention to detail on probability and other branches of mathematics doesn't mean that philosophy and sociology (I'm serious on that one, the number of players and the environment of play, such as Settlers of Catan encouraging odd temporary mini-alliances) don't have a large impact on the subjective quality of a game.

In reality, adjectives like zero-sum, solved, balanced, deep can always be quantified objectively, albeit with subjective or comparative thresholds. "Best" and "Good" will always be subjective by definition, which hugely increases the scope in how to achieve them in a given game design.
