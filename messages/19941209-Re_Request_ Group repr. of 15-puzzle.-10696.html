Martin wrote:


Hi, 
I'm wondering if somebody knows a mathematical group representation of
the famous 15-puzzle (the one under the apple symbol on every Mac).
E.g. the 2x2x2 Rubik's cube can be represented by a subgroup of the
permutation group of 21 symbols. This is done in one of the examples
in the release 3 distribution of Maple.

The purpose is, e.g., to show that some of the configurations are
impossible to obtain. 


Every position in the 15-puzzle is an permutation of 16 symbols (the empty 
square is the 16th), so its a subgroup of a permutation group (big surprise
due to Cayley's theorem :-) Each move is either an exchange between one brick
to the left or right of the hole and the hole, or an exchange between
the brick above or below. These are even permutations, so if the starting
state is an even permutation (as it is in the normal game), it will always
be an even permutation. This shows that odd permutations cannot happen.

At least this is what I vaguely remember from Martin Gardner's books. 
I might be completely wrong here, it was some time ago...
