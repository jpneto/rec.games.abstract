I am living in Germany and Morris (called Mühle) could be called our
true national game because in Germany it is more popular than Chess or
Dame (German Draughts). We have many very strong players and even
tournaments. I am probably one of the strongest players in my city.
I speak of traditional German Nine Men Morris.

Among strong players Morris games end almost always in a draw so that the
first prize in Morris tournaments is almost always shared by several
players. There are even Morris tournaments for the blind.

Two of Germany's leading players are Hans Schürmann and Manfred
Nüschler. They wrote in 1980 the book: "So gewinnt man Mühle" (How to win
Morris). This is one of the best books ever written about Morris. They
show that there is no first move advantage. In fact, the second player has
an advantage because it is more important where to place the last stone
than the first stone in the opening. A player who makes a Mühle
(three-in-a-row) rather early usually loses the game! Morris is a game of
position and strategy and you must try to control at least two of the four
four-four-intersections of the board or you will lose.
Pentagon is like "Fünfeck-Mühle" (five corner morris) and Armenian Morris
mixed together. Armenian Morris gives a sure win for the first player
while Fünfeck-Mühle has a big second move advantage. Perhaps Pentagon
balances both drawbacks so that both players have an equal chance to win.

I would like to see more advanced German Morris variants like "Windmühle"
or "Rautenmühle" to be more used in tournaments. These two variants are
much more difficult to play and both are rather old.
