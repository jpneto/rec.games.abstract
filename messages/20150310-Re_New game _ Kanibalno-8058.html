Fwiw, hyperchess came about because I was having trouble with linear differential equations - found out I was visualizing my math, and couldn't visualize the 4th dimension. I'm into simple rules that give complex games, if you consider chess and abstract strategy wargames to be reasonably complex. So it depends on your definition of "simple". I think Border War, the first CaM design that crossed the border from chess variant into wargame, to be simple, and there is a VASSAL implementation for that. It uses 5 different piece types, none of which moves more than 3 squares/turn. Leaders activate pieces within 2 squares of themselves (including themselves) and move 1 or 2 squares. They may change direction between the first and 2nd step. Infantry steps 1 in any (of the 8) directions. Skirmishers move like knights, and jump anythin in their way. Cavalry moves up to 3 squares orthogonally. Cannons move up to 3 squares diagonally. SImple chess moves. Capture is by replacement - move onto an opposing piece with a legal move and stop there, and the opposing piece is removed from the game. Terrain is very abstract. Pieces (except skirmishers) stop next to terrain they wish to enter, and move into terrain squares at the rate of 1 square/turn. Cannon can't move into trees. Cavalry can't move into hills - a standard heavy cavalry horse could not move effectively up or down a slope of over 7 degrees. And that, in brief, is all the basic rules. An amazing number of things just fall out of those basic rules. Players MUST keep a lot of reserves. Terrain offers a strong defensive position. Etc, etc. Very simple and streamlined, but it simulates a wargame better than a lot of wargames do!
