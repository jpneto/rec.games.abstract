The obvious answer is "because the board gradually disappears,
eventually leaving the kings no refuge". Every time a king moves out
of check or makes a capture, or moves with all three pieces on the
board, one square disappears. Additionally a player can choose to
refrain from re-entering because it is an option, not an obligation.

Actually you're right in that is not 'hard finitude'. Soft finitude
goes from the premiss that at least one of the players wants to win.
Stalemate is excluded. Three-fold is implemented to resolve tactical
impasses that doubtlessly can be constructed. In Shakti they
occasionally occur in actual play. In Caïssa they hardly ever do (I
can't remember even one game ending in a draw). Cyclix likely
gravitates towards Caïssa in this respect.

So a further difference with MQ is that 'finite' may be too strictly
formulated. But barring 3-fold the game will end naturally in one
player winning.
