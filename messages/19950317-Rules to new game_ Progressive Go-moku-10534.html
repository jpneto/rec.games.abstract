Progressive Go-moku
Invented by Edward Jackman

Play is on a 15x15 board. BLACK starts by placing one black stone on
the center point. WHITE the plays two stones anywhere on the board,
BLACK then plays 3 stones, WHITE plays 4, etc. All stones placed by one
player during a single turn must be mutually non-adjacent -- that is, 
they may not touch diagonally or orthoganally. First player to get 5 or
more stones in a row in any direction wins.

When playing, the player enters her/his current turns marks in 
capitals to distinguish from the previous marks and to avoid breaking
the 'mutually non-adjacent' rule. The opponent would replace all capitals
with lower case before taking her/his turn.

To play on a Go or Pente board in person, it would be helpful to have at 
least three colors of stones, the odd color being used for the current turn,
then replaced once the turn is completed.

Although this game was decide by blunder, that is rare. Usually one player
will be able to make a combination of threats that cannot all be legally 
blocked. For example:

 5  . . . . . . S . . . . . . . .
 4  . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .
 3  . . . . S . S . S . . . . . .
 2  . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .
 1  . . . . . . S . . . . . . . .
 
  a b c d e f g h i j k l m n o

In this configuration, a stone must be placed at either g3 or i3 in
order to block the winning row fghij-3. But in order to block the 
formation of the winning row 12345-h, a stone must be placed at 
h2 or h4. Unfortunately, a stone at h2 or h4 makes it illegal to place
one at g3 or i3, and vice versa.


Any comments welcome.

If you want to play this game email, play two games at once, one as black,
one as white. First move in each game is forced in the center. Make
your first as white on the second board. I'm using my initial for my
stones -- use yours for yours.
