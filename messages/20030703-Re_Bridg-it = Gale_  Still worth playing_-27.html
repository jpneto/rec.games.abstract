Mark mentions some variants that can be played using a Bridgit board and
pieces, and speculates that one-move equalization would not save the
standard game. He is right. Bridgit is a special case of the more general
Shannon edge switching game. A simple methodology has been discovered for
finding the winning move in any winning position in this game. This is
discussed in "Winning Ways for your Mathematical Plays" (I forget which
volume, sorry.) At any rate, even with three-move equalization, the
methodology would still apply, and the game is not interesting. This

method

is a bit more involved than the pairing diagram shown in the above link,

but

it is still simple enough for a computer program to play perfectly.
