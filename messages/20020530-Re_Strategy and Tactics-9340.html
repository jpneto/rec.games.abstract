I've always thought of strategy to be what you do without making reference
to individual units/pieces in a game. Take chess for example: I could say

I

have a defensive strategy, or an offensive strategy, or a strategy to
control the center of the board, etc. But as soon as I talk about

individual

pieces, like what I like to do with my queenside rook, then it's tactics.

The same holds true for wargames. In Bitter Woods (about the Battle of the
Bulge), I can talk about what rivers I want to hold, what towns I'll give

up

without a fight, or where I'd like my front lines to be by turn 4. That's
all strategy. When I start to say that such-and-such a corps needs to be

to

hex 2014 by turn 3, then that's tactics.

Take go: my strategy would be how loosely I arrange my pieces at first, or
whether I immediately attack an opponent's corner, or something like that.
Tactics would be how I position individual pieces (making reference to the
intersections, for example).

It's all a question of granularity. I think the scope of the game counts

for

a lot. What might be strategic in Bitter Woods is too granular for even a
discussion of tactics in Axis and Allies. So the game's scope is a big
determinant of what will be classed as strategy and what tactics.

Hope this helps!

CND
