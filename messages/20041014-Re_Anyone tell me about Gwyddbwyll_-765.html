Generally most sources I've read seem to associate Fidchell with tafl-style games, Although some do connect it to Ludus latrunculorum, so it is certainly subject to debate. To add to the confusion, according to game historian David Parlett, by the 15th century the Gaelic word Fidchell had commonly become adopted to refer to Chess. In the modern Irish spelling, Ficheall can now be used as a generic for any kind of wooden board game. In fact, in the Wooing of Etain, quoted below, the translators actually used the word Chess rather than "Fidchell."

 What is thy name? said Eochaid.
Not famous, said he, Midir of Bri Leith.
What has brought thee? said Eochaid.
To play Fidchell with thee, said he.
Of a truth I am good at Fidchell, said Eochaid.
Let us make trial of it, said Midir.
The queen is asleep, said Eochaid, and it is in her house that the Fidchell board is.
I have here, said Midir, a Fidchell board that is not inferior. That was true: a silver board and golden men, and each corner there lit up by precious stones, and a bag for the men of plaited links of bronze.
-The Wooing of Etain Translated by Osborn Bergin, and Richard Irvine Best, 1937.

Here's a little more on Gwyddbwyll from my reading

Gwyddbwyll, and Tawlbyrdd are both referred to in Welsh sagas. Fortunately a manuscript from 1587 in the Welsh National Library solves the mystery in regard to Tawlbwrdd. The description is unmistakably a Tafl-style game set on an 11x11 grid: The king in the center of the board and twelve men in the place next to him, and twenty-four lie in wait to capture him -with the 24 attackers equally spaced on the four edges of the board. The term Gwyddbwyll seems to be older than Tawlbwrdd. Part of the theory in regard to Latrunculorum is that Gwyddbwyll was its Welsh counterpart, and that Tawlbwrdd may have been a Tafl game imported by Norse and/or Saxon invaders and gradually replaced Gwyddbwyll.

Here's a few other British/Celtic game names that are a puzzle to modern researchers.
"Brandubh" (Black Raven) appears in the Irish sagas as early as the 12th century, one such source describes the use of 5 men against 8. This would seem well-suited to a small Tafl-like game played on a small grid like the Ballinderry board, or similar engraved boards found on the Orkneys.

"Buanfach," "Cennchaem," "Conchobair," "Ard-ri"

In the end, we have to remember that these were probably folk games whose rules could vary from one village to the next. We only have to look at the dozens of versions of Wari/Mancala to wonder if it is possible that all the names above were simply regional terms for just one or two games or their variants.
