In article <3beiho$e7g@panix.com> rhk@panix.com (Sator Arepo) writes:

csc112@nof.abdn.ac.uk (r.boswell) writes:

Logically, I should have thought that a pawn that moves two squares forward
would have to be captured on the third rank of the board from which it
started, rather than the board on which it ends up.  Is this correct?



	Since the pawn to be captured has made exactly one move, it would
have to be captured on the second board if it is to be captured e.p.
After the capture, the capturing pawn winds up on the home board.


I agree with rhk; it seems to me that, logically, en passant is capturing
the "virtual pawn" that moved one space instead of two -- and hence is on the
second board, just behind the real pawn.  In other words, white moves
e2(home)-e4(second), and black moves d4(second)-e3(home), which captures the
white pawn on e4(second).

The trouble is that e3(second) might already be occupied, in which case
black's move either makes two captures at once (if the occupant is white)
or is illegal (if black).


	For postal play in the NOST (in which many Chess Variants are
played), en passant is often forbidden for variants, because keeping it
is often more trouble than it's worth.


I think that's the best decision here.  But then, I never really liked
the en passant rule anyway; it's a kludgy solution to a non-problem caused
by a rule change that shouldn't have been made in the first place.
