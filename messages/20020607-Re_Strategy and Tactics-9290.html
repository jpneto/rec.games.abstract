FYI, the version of Deep Blue that defeated Kasparov did *NOT*
make any sacrifices.


Whenever you allow an opponent to capture a piece, with the knowledge
that you can immediately recapture the opposing piece and thus
re-equalize the material balance, you have made a "sacrifice": you have
traded a material disadvantage (after the opponent's capture, you have
less material than the opponent) for a positional advantage (your
position is actually better than the material balance would suggest,
because you have the immediate opportunity to capture an opposing piece
which is not adequately defended).


I meant "sacrifice" in the usual sense as used by chessplayers;
that is, a sacrifice of material where you can't see the eventual
regain of material but instead evaluate the positional factors
(e.g. gain in time, opening lines of attack, etc.) of the resulting
position as being more important than the material.  I certainly
did not mean sacrifice in the sense of a "sacrifice" that occurs
in a tactical combination.



The only difference between one sound "sacrifice", and another, is how
long it takes before the material balance is restored.  In some cases
it's only 1 ply, in other cases it might be 10 ply, in other cases it
might be 40 ply.  It has to happen eventually, if the sacrifice is
sound.


That's sort of my original point.  The increasing search depth achieved

You are reading *FAR* too much into a single sentence of mine.
Kasparov probably was under the delusion that the weaker aspects
of computer chess programs would always enable him to win.  I
knew it was inevitable that a computer chess program would eventually
defeat the world champion.  In fact in 1990, I publicly said that
it would happen before the end of the decade.  Generally speaking,
even grandmasters can't look ahead very far at all compared to
a computer program.  The long term strategic advantages of the
top human chess players gets superseded by the repeated shorter term
advantages of a computer program.  There is more than one way to
play chess.
