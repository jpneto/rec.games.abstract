On Jun 29, 1:27 pm, Mark Steere <markste...@gmail.com> wrote:

Fractal is a variant of Hex.

http://www.marksteeregames.com/pictures/Fractal.jpg

In Hex, the center cells are valued much more highly than those in the
outlying areas. Fractal attempts to compensate for this disparity by
shrinking the center area cells and expanding the outer area cells,
and by shearing the sharp points off Hex's rhombus.

The design goal in Fractal was a more level playing field, a more
equitable game.  Fractal presents a finely scaled array of values for
first moves.  Player 1 can select a move much closer to zero in value
than he can in Hex, severely weakening Player 2's pie rule advantage.

   I've worked up a variation on the original Fractal board, It can be
found here:

http://transform.to/~cubist/stuff/fractal_board.2.gif

   The board-variant is in the upper left. To the lower right, is the
basis of my variant. What's truly interesting about it is that if you
make a copy of the template, shrink it down by a factor of 1 / (sqrt
(3)), and rotate it 90 degrees, the copy will fill up the hole in the
center of the template! So it should be possible to just keep on going
'inside', deeper and deeper, endlessly.
   Obviously, an 'infinite' board like that could never be implemented
as a physical boardgame. But it *could* be implemented as a *computer*
game! If anyone does so, it might be a good idea to let the user/
player specify how 'deep' the board is -- how many templates 'down' it
goes before it bottoms out into an array of normal hexagons.
