"The tessellation is an afterthought" is both untrue and ridiculous. If
anyone but you had suggested it you'd surely have laughed.


The rules to Hex -- as well as most of your games -- explicitly call for hex
grid, and for good reason. It is the only trivalent grid on a plane in which
each cell has an identical neighborhood and a single orientation. Adding the
FCFC rule allows for only one other -- the square grid.


You have struggled to port some games that work in one tessellation to
another within this very newsgroup, demonstrating to me that you don't think
it's an afterthought.


While it is true that the *object*, and in some cases, the *strategies* of
connection games may be demonstrated with a blank sheet of paper and pen,
this is usually NOT true of the *tactics*. Maybe because you -- as you have
claimed yourself -- are uninterested in actual game play, only game
invention, you vastly underestimate the importance of tactics in defining a
game.
(For a connection game that works quite will with NO grid: see CALCULUS for
one example: (http://www.boardgamegeek.com/game/30914))


In my opinion, from a game player's perspective, tactics are the *more*
important feature. It is my experience and belief that a strong Hex player,
having never heard of The Game of Y or Poly-Join or Atoll or any of a large
number of hexagonally tessellated connection games, could play these games
at a high level instantly. This is *not* the case when moving to a
non-trivalent grid.


And although those *subtle* differences between playing hex on a standard
hexelated rhombus and on a rhombus of pentagons+heptagons squares+octagons
or other trivalent grid are not as overwhelming as when playing on a
non-trivalent grid, the greater the players' experience, the more the
pronounced those differences become. To a beginner, the difference between
FIDE Chess and Chess with the back row pieces in randomized positions is
negligable.


That Atoll is "fundamentally different from hex" because it has 8 (or more)
goals rather than 4 is an odd assertion for you to make. If it *were* true,
it would seem to undercut the point you're trying to make. A generalization
is not "fundamentally different* from one of it specific cases. The
fundamental goal of hex is to connect one's goal areas. The number of goals
is no more or less a fundamental change than the way the pieces connect.
