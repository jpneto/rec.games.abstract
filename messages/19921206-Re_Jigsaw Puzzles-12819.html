In article <1992Dec4.220321.18150@husc15.harvard.edu> blom@husc15.harvard.edu writes:


Does the complexity of solving a jigsaw puzzle increase on the order of the
square of the number of pieces in the puzzle?


It seems that this is related to the guessing game you asked about.
trunc(log2(100)) = 6, so 7 yes-no questions suffice for a person to guess on
number in 100.  That is, to find one thing in 100 requires log2(100)=6.64
bits.

Suppose we have a square jigsaw puzzle of side n.  The orientation of each
piece is a choice of 1 in 4, or 2 bits.  Suppose we start with a corner and
want to place pieces successively, and that the pieces are unique so that we
know when we have one.  This requires 2n^2 bits.  Now to chose the next piece
is a choice of 1 in n^2, so there is an additional log(n^2) = 2log(n) bits.
Interestingly, the NEXT piece must be chosen using log(n^2-1) bits, the third
is log(n^2-3) etc.  Clearly the answer is the sum of these.  If you want the
average per piece, and if the puzzle is linear, a very beautiful result drops
out, which I will leave you to find.  The 2 dimensional case is a hair
trickier, but gives similar results...
