A lot of arguments have been made that abstract games can contain *no*
physical skill, which is (IMHO) a bit ludicrous.  Pieces must be moved
correctly, and that requires some degree of dexterity/hand-eye coordination.
Mistakes can cause setbacks or even losses.  A better case (IMO) would be
that in abstract games, mental skills should predominate over physical
skills, which applies to Crokinole.

Arguments have also been made that an abstract game should be able to be
played by someone who directs another (or a machine) on where to place a
piece (reducing the reliance on dexterity in order to play the game).  This
can also apply to Crokinole.  I have personally "coached" many novice
players through a game, never touching a disc, but describing to the novice
where to shoot, what to aim for, how much force to use, etc., which often
results in that person/team winning the game.

The real skill in Crokinole is not in the hand-eye coordination of being
able to fillip a disc accurately (execute a shot,) but in being able to
"see" potential shots, weigh their risks/rewards, avoid pitfalls, use the
board and your opponents' weaknesses to your advantage, etc., etc.

I can teach most people to be able to flick a disc competently in about a
half hour or less.  It takes *much* longer to teach them how to look for,
see, and analyze positions & shots on the board.
