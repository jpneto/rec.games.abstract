 it is difficult to back up the assertion, either way, for
 a sufficiently complex abstract game.  you need an enormous
 sample size.


 i am curious why there arent simply games with one move for the
 first player and then 2 moves back and forth the rest of the way.


 progressive chess goes 1 move, 2 moves, 3 moves, 4, 5, 6, 7...
 has there been any research on 1, 2, 2, 2, 2, 2...?  it isnt
 clear to me was the progressive chess tempo advantage is.  if
 just count the cumulative moves, it goes 1-0, 1-2, 4-2, 4-6,
 9-6, 9-12, 16-12, 16-20....but defending for an 8-move turn
 must be a nightmare.  with 1,2,2,2,2...the analysis paralysis
 problem may be held under control.  however, this fairy chess
 version may have already been shown to lead to an unsatisfactory
 game in the past.


 my approach to this problem was different.  i split up the move
 into 2 parts, with each player having control over one of the 2
 parts.  it's sort of like baseball beginning with the ball in
 the pitching hand of the defense.


 certainly there must have been a fairy chess game where your
 opponent gets to pick which moveable piece you must use.  i
 would suspect that would need a lot of fixups to actually work,
 without thinking to hard about it.


 however, to return to Hex and its variations, this "pitching"
 approach cant apply unless the board is multicolored and you
 are given a color you have to play your move on.  Hex naturally
 leads to 3 colors, but you could have any number.  despite
 seemingly having control, the defense will eventually have to
 pick a losing color.
