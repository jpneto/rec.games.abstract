 > Alternative tessellations for connection games have long been

I didn't say or imply otherwise. However, non-trivalent grids have generally
been ignored. The point of my post was that by using the first come, first
connected rule (FCFC), Hex, Y and other games can be ported to ANY
tessellation, *not merely those that are trivalent*. Non-trivalent grids
have been overlooked or used only with extra rules to deal with deadlocks
that change them from pure connection games -- Onyx for example -- or change
the underlying geometry back to trivalent -- Quax and Zen for example. By
adding the obvious FCFC rule to Hex, Y and a myriad of other games, play on
Cairo, Square, Triangular and other tessellations without deadlocks or draws
is enabled.

An alternative tessellation does not a Hex generalization make.

An alternative tessellation does a Hex *variant* make.
A ruleset that allows for the choice of tessellations as part of the game
*does* a Hex generalization make.
Nothing in the ordinal rules of Hex, Y or Atoll implies that non-hex grids
are included. This goes triple for non-trivalent grids, which *clearly*
weren't even hinted at by the original rules of Hex or Atoll.
Both Piet Hein and John Nash defined the game Hex (aka Con-Tac-Tix aka
Polygon aka Nash aka John, etc) as being played on a rhombus shaped board of
hexagons (or its dual) with exactly 4 goal areas. A game -- say Anyboard Hex
-- defined with all the rules of Hex with the addition of giving the players
choice swapping a hex board for any non-hexagonal and/or non-trivalent
tilings is by definition a generalization of Hex.

 > Hex is the trivial, special case of "Atoll-Loophole"
Hex is also a special case of an infinite number of possible
generalizations.
To illustrate:
A) The game of Hex was defined by both Nash and Hein as being played on a
field of hexagons.
B) Define �Anyboard Trivalent Hex� as Hex with one added rule -- players
create any trivalent board.
C) Define �Anyboard Hex� as Hex with one added rule -- players create any
board. If it is non-trivalent, the FCFC rule is used.
D) Insert rules for generalized Atoll/Loophole.
Using the rules of either B, C or D, players may decide to use a standard
11x11 rhombus of hexagons with the standard four edges as goals. The result
is the same, standard game of Hex -- a special case of any of the three
generalizations. Each ruleset allows a different but overlapping set of
possible games to be played. Combine the generalizations of C and D  and you
get a broader generalization of Hex.

And if it makes you happy you can play Atoll-Loophole on an unlimited
variety of tessellations, although that does nothing to further generalize
what was already a tessellation independent game.
