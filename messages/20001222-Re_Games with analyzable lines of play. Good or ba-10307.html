The RULES of bridge are well defined. When bidding which bids are legal
is well defined. When playing which cards are legal is well defined.
What is not covered by the rules are conventions -- both during bidding
and card play.  I tend to equate the conventions to be components of the
strategy and tactics of bridge.  The fact that bidding conventions exist
is acknowledged by the rule requiring the partner of the bidder to
explain the meaning of the bid if the opponents ask.

It is the usually accepted thing to do. I did play with psychic fiends,
though, and I had substantially more information about their tendencies
than the simple warning conveyed. In particular, that they did not make a
psychic bid in certain circumstances carried enough information to be
alertable, in my opinion. Our solution was to restrict the psychics
somewhat and turn them into a convention, which meant that I had to do
them, too.

For those who do not play enough bridge to know what a psychic bid is, my
definition is a bid that willfully and grossly violates the accepted
conventions of the partnership, that is, a lie to your partner and your
opponents. For example, if to open 1 spade promises 5 spades and a decent
hand, then it would be a psychic bid if I said it with 2 spades and a weak
hand.

Bridge assumes a high level of ethics. However, a partnership used to
playing a bad but complicated set of conventions will have an unfair
advantage over those who are not familiar with the proper defenses
against those conventions. Thankfully, there is a lot more to bridge
than exploiting strange conventions, but I would like to see at least
some tournaments have no restrictions or alerts on the bidding
whatsoever beyond constraints on passing extra information.


The ethics here is that having an undisclosed (to the opponents)
convention is considered cheating.  There is no trival way to prove that
a partnership is in fact using an undisclosed convention.


The fact is that the amount of information a player has about his/her
partner's tendencies or any complicated unusual convention exceeds what can
be explained to the opponents in a reasonable amount of time. I don't think
how much information is supposed to be divulged is well-defined.

Of course, there are many other terms that are not well-defined that do not
bother me, such as "hesitation."
