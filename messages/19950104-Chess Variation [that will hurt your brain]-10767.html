<h1>Phase Chess -- Intro and General Rules</h1>

<p>
While experimenting with possible scenarios of 4-dimensional tic-tac-toe[1],
my mind made a big phase shift into (what promises to be) a nifty four
person chess variation.  Here's the low-down as it occurred to me, and
a few thoughts on it after playing one game.

<p>
I kicked around several names for this, mostly centered around the
idea that you're playing twice at once.  But, I think my friend Dave
was right.... in saying... 'Phase Chess'.  After playing for even
a few moves, you'll see how your partner's pieces phase in and out of
your play.

<h2>Original Concept</h2>
<p>
This requires four players and four boards (w/full chess pieces on each).
<em>[Actually, if you're really together, you only need two sets of pieces,
but I highly recommend four sets of pieces (and if you're really slick
you may want 8 sets of pieces if you can distinguish sets, but that's
for you to decide).]</em>

<p>
The players sit in a square.  Opposite players both play the same
color and are both playing against adjacent players.  For example:

<pre>
    Player 1 --- white vs. black --- Player 2
       |                                |
     white                            black
      vs.                              vs.
     black                            white
       |                                |
    Player 4 --- black vs. white --- Player 3
</pre>

<p>
Player's one and three start.  Each player must make the same move in
both games he/she's playing.  If a move isn't legal in one of your games,
it isn't legal.  An example opening would be...

<pre>
	player1 player3 player2 player4
    1.  e4	d3	e5	N-c6
</pre>

<p>
The three immediately obvious variations center around what to do
with dead pieces.
<ul>
<li> a) if it's dead in one of your games, it's dead in both.
(One of the big thrills of the game is being able to take four pieces
out of play at the same time.  And, if your opponents are in "phase",
it may even be possible for you and your partner to remove eight
opponent pieces in one turn.)
<li> b) if it's dead in one of your games, it's stuck in the other.
(you can't move it cuz you can't mirror it)
<li>c) if it's dead in one of your games, it's free in the other
(you can move it at the expense of making no move in your other game)
</ul>

<p>
Big note: It's easy to be in checkmate based on both games, but not on either
on its own.  The really tricky scene is when it looks like you're in
check, but your partners pieces are blocking your opponent from fully
attacking.  We had one wicked setup with a
stand-off involving three knights and two queens.  One knight on one
of my opponents boards was blocking me from taking my other opponent's
queen.  The positioning of one of my partner's knights was preventing
the other queen from taking mine, and even if it had, it would have
been a trade of pieces because I had a knight guarding my queen.  I
suppose you'd have to see it.  But, you'll get the basic point in
under a half-hour.

<h2>Caveats When We Played</h2>
<p>
First off, we chose version 'a', for simplicity <em>(or so we thought)</em>.
My partner and I got into all sorts of situations where we made the
mistake of having him kill a piece that was going to protect my move.
When the piece disappeared from one of my boards, we both ended up
hosed.  Our opponents fell into this once, but we almost missed it
and ended up with an odd number of dead pieces (a "dead" giveaway).

<p>
Pawns were almost worthless.  They couldn't attack, unless they could
attack in both games <em>(easy to avoid)</em>.  They were more of a nuisance in
that you could never realize your rooks until some of the pawns were out
of commission.

<p>
Teamwork was of utmost importance.  The person opposite you
<strong>greatly</strong> affects your games.  This isn't a problem.
This, in fact, is the one of the major highlights of the game.
But, we hadn't decided on timing issues before we got underway.
We got into several situations where, if I moved, and then my partner
moved, we'd both take a piece.  But, if he moved, then I moved,
only one of us would take a piece.

<p>
After we noticed this problem, we decided that both black moves should
happen simultaneously, and both white moves should happen simultaneously.

<p>
The problem that then arose, was: <em>could you move into check at the same
time your partner was blocking that check for you?</em>  We eventually,
ditched sheer simultaneous play into, if it didn't matter whether you or your
partner went first (in terms of points and check), then you could play
it in either order.  I don't think that's going to be the proper way
for two reasons: 1) it's hard to sort all of that out, and 2) order
would still affect the final board position.

<p>
So, it's clear that the 'order' rule becomes a very key element of this
game.

<h2>Suggestions for better play</h2>
<p>
Make pawns be able to attack diagonally even if it's only taking in
one of the boards.  <em>(If this makes them too powerful, then maybe
make it a suicide attack -- it takes but dies??).</em>

<p>
Fix ordering question by:
<ul>
<li> a) freely allow team-non-synchronous moves
<em>(could severely lessen the team scheming here with clocks)</em>
<li> b) enforcing team-synchronous moves [moving into check being defined
as it would be if you had to move before your partner]
<em>(takes away some of the havoc you and partner can create)</em>
<li> c) take turns player 1, then 2, then 3, then 4...
<em>(but this takes away much of the "team" aspect)</em>
</ul>

<p>
It may also be interesting to play <strong>blind</strong>
(in which you don't get to see any boards but the two you're playing on).
Or to play <strong>mute</strong>, open eyes, no cross-talk.

<p>
I'm interested in playing the 6-person/9-board/3-game-at-time
scenario, but I'm not sure that I'll ever get that much interest,
equipment, and time together.

<p>
We played for four and a half hours last night before we bailed
in favor of playing some other time with a pre-defined order rule and
something better for pawns.
