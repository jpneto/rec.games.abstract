"Edward D. Collins" wrote:


I've invented a new game called "Quack". It's
exactly like chess, except when you checkmate
someone, you reset the board, and replay, until
someone has acheived three checkmates -- first
person to do so wins.



If you have a 75% chance of beating someone at
chess, then you have a 90% chance of beating
them at Quack.



If you have a 64% chance of beating
someone at chess, then you have a 75%
chance of beating them at Quack.


Both of those figures sound about right.  Obviously, if you have a 50%
chance of beating them at chess, you'll still have a 50% chance of
beating them at Quack.  However, this changes at this point.  A player
with a 51% chance will have a slightly greater than 51% chance of
beating them at Quack.  What's the formula?


As you say below, it's a best-of-5 game. So if the
probability of winning at chess is p, then the
probability of winning at Quack (draws ignored) is

   10 p^3 (1-p)^2           (3-2)
 +  5 p^4 (1-p)             (4-1)
 +    p^5                   (5-0)

= p^3 (10 - 20p + 10p^2 + 5p - 5p^2 + p^2)
= p^3 (10 - 15p + 6p^2)



...So, for every level in chess, there
are about two levels in Quack. Quack
is therefore much more complicated than
chess.


Hmmmmnn... let me think about this.  My first reaction is that "Quack"
isn't more 'complicated' at all.  Quack is simply a best 3-out-of-5 game
chess match.  (The winner is the first person to win three games.)  And
thus being the same game, it would be equally as complicated.
