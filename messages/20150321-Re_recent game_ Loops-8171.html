How I understood the rules, a loopless game couldn't happen in Loops, even between cooperating players.  I interpreted a loop to be a group of like-colored, interconnected stones which include at least two perimeter stones.  For example, two like-colored, adjacent perimeter stones would by themselves form a loop.  Even if you required that a loop's two perimeter stones be widely separated, the principles of Hex (or Y if you prefer) would guarantee that one player would have that.  And yes, you can form filled-in loops.  Why not?  The end of the game comes when the board is completely filled, or equivalently, when no new loops can be formed.  There's no need for passing.  

Assuming Red always moves first:

Scenario A: The left side of the board is solid blue, and the right side solid red.  Red wins by one point.

Scenario B: The left side of the board is solid blue, except for one red stone embedded in the blue mass, and the right side solid red.  Blue wins by one point.

Like I said earlier, every point will be owned by one player or the other, precluding draws.


In such a situation neither player would want to place pieces and they would both pass (which is how the game originally ended), but of course they might pass with an even number of spaces occupied. 


I mean, it's your game, but I don't see any reason to make it Go-like by allowing passing.  In fact, after some consideration, I do *not* consider Loops to be a Go variant, in spite of the obvious, but superficial, similarities.  Loops isn't captureless Go with loop rules tacked on.  The loop rules are the essence of Loop, and there's no need to involve Go principles.
