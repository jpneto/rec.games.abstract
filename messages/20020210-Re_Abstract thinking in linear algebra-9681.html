Another digression:



Teaching reading by the whole word method, besides wasting years,
seems to have reduced or even destroyed the ability of a substantial

My siblings and I learned to read by "the whole word method" (also
called "see and say").  So did my younger son.  Possibly my other
children as well, and I'm certain it had a great influence on them.


I suspect you quickly developed phonic awareness.  Most who
learned this way not only did not, but became resistant to it.


The evidence (as explained to me:  I am not an expert on language
acquisition) is that "self-taught" readers overwhelmingly use the whole
word method.  You just read to the children a lot and suddenly they
read.  Frankly, speaking works the same way (in my experience).


There is considerable evidence to the contrary.  The scientific
evidence is that children pick up grammar before much vocabulary,
by age 3, and some before age 1.  They are able to recognize 
regularities.

Self-taught readers also look for the regularities, thus using
some form of phonics quickly.  One learns the more used words
as whole words by using them, but learning by the whole word
method gives no basis on which to build.


However, it isn't really a method.  As you say, it is a terrible way to
_teach_ reading.  Strangely, this is one of the few instances where
pedagogical orthodoxy followed research.  Studies showed that people
who learned to read by the "whole word method" were better readers than
those who learned by "the phonics method". 


At the time it was introduced, this was not "known", and it has
never been established.  The books which came out in the 1950s
showed that.

What was observed was that the people who recognized more whole
words, and even larger units, read better.  I recognize 65536
as being 2^16, but never set out to "learn" this.  

It was even observed that good readers took in several words at
a glance, and the whole word method was the closest available.
This was confusing the I/O channel with the central processor.
