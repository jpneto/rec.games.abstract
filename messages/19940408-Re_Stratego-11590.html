In response to Marco (mwaaije@.cs.vu.nl), Bruno (bruno@cerberus.csd.uwm.edu)
writes about Stratego:


It's sold as a children's/family game here. It is sold in a lot of places.
Occasionally it is advertised on TV.


There is also a commercial version of the software that was licensed by
Milton Bradley to, I think, Accolade.  It allowed for several levels of
difficulty, which an experience Stratego player should have no problem
beating.  It allowed for a number of "house rules" such as whether the
winning piece replaced the losing piece's location; and whether ties
went to the attacker.  These change the playing strategy completely.


I have found that the game strongly favors defending rather than attacking.


Somewhat true.  There is a lot of deception that can go on when playing
each of the different rule sets; however, my tactics include slipping a
Major or Colonel into the back lines and hacking away at the smaller
pieces until you force your opponent to commit a large piece to the defense
or you locate a possible flag "enclave".  It's amazing how many quick
wins you can get when you commit yourself to the offense, especially when
you have the other "channels" of attack and/or retreat blocked.


When both players feel this way the game is unplayable. This may have

something

to do with why the game isn't treated that seriously (at least by the game

players I know).

This is true.  If both players choose defense, it's a boring game.  But...
like I said above, a person that plays defense can get his pieces blocked
in (as he can't move though his own pieces) and, through the loss of 
mobility, not be able to respond to a coordinated attack.  And of course, 
if you always surround your flag with bombs, killing off all your 
opponents miners kinda gives you tremendous operational flexibility.
