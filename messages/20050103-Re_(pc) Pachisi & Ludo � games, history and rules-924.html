You might be interested to know that the Chinese version of ludo is called
Fei Xing Qi - literally 'flying chess' but often translated as 'aeroplane
chess'. The pieces are often little wooden or plastic discs with pictures of
aeroplanes on them. Most traditionally, sets made in China come in little
cardboard boxes with wooden pieces, a very simple die and a thin paper board
that folds several times before it fits in the box. Newer sets use plastic
and in the last year or so, I have noticed that sets with little plastic
aeroplanes have become available.

A distinctive feature is that the spaces on the board are also coloured - if
a player lands on a space of that player's own colour, that piece can jump
forward to the next space of the same colour. The spaces at the inner
corners of the cross are also connected to a space on the opposite side
across the arms of the cross - a player landing on his/her own colour can
fly across the arm of the cross to the other side.
