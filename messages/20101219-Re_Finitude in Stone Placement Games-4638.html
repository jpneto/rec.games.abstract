7x7 Hex (without the pie rule) would be non-robust.  With advancing skill,
the game will eventually be swamped with first move advantage.  Even with
the pie rule, if the board is small enough it will be ruined by second move
advantage.

Robustness and scalability are related concepts.  Is a game even scalable
and, if so, how finely scalable?  In Hex, as with many games, to combat
first move advantage you increase the size of the board.  You can't just add
another row and create a long quadrilateral.  You have to jump up to the
next larger rhombus.

7x7=49 cells ---> 8x8=64 cells.  A 30% increase.

It could be worse though.  You could be required to jump up to the next
larger *odd* size rhombus.

7x7=49 cells ---> 9x9=81 cells.  A 65% increase.

In Oust you can jump up to the next size board.  Even or odd, or shape,
don't matter in a game of total annihilation.  Yay annihilation.  In Rive,
you have to jump up to the next odd sized board, since it's a territory
game, but board shape doesn't matter so you can just add an extra row.

I think you're jumping the gun here,


:)  This has all been musing on my part and is by no means intended as any
sort of design how-to.

Consider the following "game".  Black and White take turns placing a single
stone of their own color onto an unoccupied cell on an odd size board. 
Whoever ends up with the most stones on the board wins.  Automatic win for
Black.  About as non-robust as you can get.

Now consider said game with sporadic, multi-stone captures interspersed. 
For one thing you get "more game for your money".  Not that economics is the
focus.  But getting more moves out of a given set of equipment combats first
move advantage simply by lengthening the game.  As a rule of thumb, a game
should be at least 60 moves.  A 60 move game will generally be a lot more
robust than a 20 move game.

Capturing combats move order advantage not just by lengthening the game
though.  It injects chaos, uncertainty about future positions.  Does this
destroy depth?  No.  Oust has as much depth as any game.  But the boardscape
can change pretty dramatically during play, and not always predictably.
