   Chess is a *static* game. By that, I mean that all pieces are

always in

the same positions at the beginning of every game. There are only two
players, and play alternates between them. A certain amount of
intelligence is required to play; but sheer, raw ability to consider

and

process a massive number of moves several turns in advance is most
necessary.
   I expect that within, probably, ten years (twenty at most), chess

will

be "solved" by computers, meaning that it can be shown with certainly

that

either white or black is guaranteed a win with proper play
(sequential-turn games usually favor the first player when "solved").

   But try a "dynamic" 18XX game (1830, 1856, 1870, or other 18XX in

which

there is no fixed order in corporation-opening): There are no pieces

on

the board at the beginning; players have only starting capital. There

are

no random elements save initial player order (which is quickly

rendered

moot during initial auctions and changeable turn order based on

company

stock valuations).
