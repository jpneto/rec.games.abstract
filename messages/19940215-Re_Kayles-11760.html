In article <2jp911$bfm@csugrad.cs.vt.edu> dbush@csugrad.cs.vt.edu (David Bush) writes:


It sounds like the first player could always win by dividing the line into
2 identical regions on the first move. If n is odd, remove the middle token.
If n is even, remove the 2 middle tokens. Now any move the 2nd player makes
is answered by the corresponding move in the other region. The 1st player
must always win with this procedure, n'est-ce pas?


While it is true that the first player always wins this game, the strategy that
you proposed does not work (depending on how you interpret "corresponding",
which I take to mean "identical").  In particular, if n= 3 or 4, then clearly
this does not work.  If n=5, then the proper 2nd move for player one is the
"opposite" of player two's first move (ie, if player two took one, player one
should take two, and vice versa).
