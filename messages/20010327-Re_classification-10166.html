Classification what is a `chess variant' seems a hard task. For many criteria,
one can come up with examples that show that the criterium is somewhat
`wrong'.

For example, if we look to the goal of the game, what about Losing chess?
Seems a `chess variant' to me? (Rules: chess, but capture obligatory, and
winning by getting rid of all your pieces; kings not royal.)

In fact, for about every criterium, you could take FIDE-chess, and modify it
only slightly just to make that criterium wrong, and often get something that
feels like a variant of chess.

Giving a classification of what is a `chess variant' thus is rather hard, and
I think it'll be impossible to find one that satisfies everybodies taste.

Only some few. Havannah is an example (connection game AND a forming

project, and I think there are only three hybrids. Don't forget that there
are final goals (which decide who has won the game; Go --> territory) and
instrumental goals (goals that help you to achieve the final goal; Go -->
connection). My classification system is only based on the final goal.

One thing we could discuss further: Draughts has a primary final goal -->
capture all opponent's men, but also a secondary final goal --> blockade
your opponent so that he cannot move. I decided to ommit this secondary
goal in my classification of board games. However, this secondary goal I
just mentioned is the primary and only final goal in some abstract games,
e.g. Juego de los Amazonas or Forge. I describe these games as a
particular sub-group of territory games (!). I think that the ability to
make moves is a subtle  "measure" of territory.
