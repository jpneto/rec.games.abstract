There are three interesting questions:


The first three have been covered in a previous post.  The fourth is tough.


 1: In chess, you may only capture en passant on the first move after the
    opponent's pawn moved.  Is this rule relaxed to allow you to wait until
    you next roll an ace?


Nope.  Next roll only.


 2: as Thomas Hayes asks: can you capture en passant a pawn that made a double
    move [rather than a two-square single move]


Nope.  Has to be the result of the two-move priveledge.


 3: generalizing 2:



     White's pawn is on E5.  Black rolls 11 and plays D7-D5-D4.  White rolls
     1X; perhaps we can let him move E5-D6*?


Yup, this was Example 2.  The capture is possible because the two pawn
moves are considered to be simultaneous with respect to en passant.


 4: If white moves a pawn two cells and puts a real piece behind the pawn can
    Black capture both?



     White has a pawn on E5.  Black rolls 12 and plays pawn D7-D5 and N-D6.
     Can white play E5-D6** and gobble up both?  Can he play E5-D6* and take
     only the knight


Yikes!  I hadn't considered this possibility at all.  Ugh.  Good question.

Hmmm...  I guess there are a few reasonable options.  Here are some ideas
that I *don't* like.  We could forbid the en passant capture in this one
case, but that is inconsistent.  We could change the en passant rules
entirely, to take order of moves into account, but that would virtually
eliminate en passant situations.  We could take out the en passant rule
entirely, which some purists may prefer anyway.

My ruling (not that I have any authority in the matter) is that *both*
pieces can be captured with the single pawn move.  In the example above,
White would play e5xd6 and capture both the knight (directly) and the
pawn (by en passant).

The justification for this rule is that, in some sense, both pieces
occupy the d6 square.  The pawn is on that square in a virtual sense,
just as it is in any en passant situation, while the knight is on that
square in the physical sense.
