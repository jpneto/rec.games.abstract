Gerry, if I understand you what you're saying, then I respectfully
disagree.

Yes, the more games you play, the better chance the stronger player will
have to prove his superiority.  This is obvious since we all know a one
game match by itself means almost nothing.  But even if two players
play, for example, a 1,000 game match, a player "one step up" in playing
ability will still only win about 75% of the games.  That's what it
means to be one step up... he's going to win about 75% of the time.  In
fact, the more games played in this match or series, the closer to the
actual 75% percentage we will get.




I've invented a new game called "Quack". It's exactly like chess, except
when you checkmate someone, you reset the board, and replay, until
someone has acheived three checkmates -- first person to do so wins. 

If you have a 75% chance of beating someone at chess, then you have a 90%
chance of beating them at Quack. If you have a 64% chance of beating
someone at chess, then you have a 75% chance of beating them at Quack.


If one is 'two' steps up one will win about 92% of the time.  


So, for every level in chess, there are about two levels in Quack. Quack
is therefore much more complicated than chess.
