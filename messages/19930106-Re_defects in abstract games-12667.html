The draw-decision question has a tradeoff --- decisiveness of a victory.

Whereas hockey is prone to draws, basketball's large amount of scoring
degrades the signifigance of any particular score.  The same distinction
is even more pronounce in chess and go.  Go works in a huge number of
tiny gradations, whereas chess has a single, definative checkmate.

Which you prefer is entirely a matter of taste, and the suggestion that
these gradations somehow make go more "complex" or that a larger number
of moves in the search tree make the game more "challenging" are just
insulting.  On the latter point, not all moves are created equal, and the
equation of placing a stone in go to the move of a pawn in chess is 
asinine, or suggest the "search tree size" is a particular bonus sounds
rather like adolescent males worrying about how their penises "measure
up" more than anything worthy of interest.

Remember that rock-paper-scissors is more complex and challenging than
both go and chess in it's own way, and has a "search tree" of three.
Also there are games with an infinitely large search tree which are
quite simply trivial.

I hope that doesn't sound like a mad chessman defending his game.  The
only reason I limited my attack to go is that while the "Go is better
than chess" contigent has reared it's ugly head, the counterpart from
chess's fan club have been thus far silent (or absent at least).

Trying to justify game preference logically is a futile proposal, and we
can do without the psuedo-logic that comes from the attempt.
