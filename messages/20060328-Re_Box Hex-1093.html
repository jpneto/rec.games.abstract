Are you referring to any specific size grid? My preferred size these
days is 19x19, which can be played on the Little Golem turn-based
server, as well as Kurnik, the most popular real-time server:

http://www.littlegolem.net
http://www.kurnik.org/


On 19x19, the advantage of the first move without swap is much less,
but it's still there. Playing without the pie rule results in a less
interesting game. I'm not strong enough to guaranteee a win
if I move first wihout swap on such a large grid, or even on a
smaller grid, but I'm very glad the pie rule is in place, because it
makes a fairer game.

Other games, such as chess for example, have a significant percentage
of draws between strong players. The first move advantage DOES have
an effect among the top-level players. It may be a matter of just a
few percentage points, but at that level of play, that's a lot.
If chess players were to adopt the pie rule, I suspect that better
than 95% of all games played between grandmasters this way would end
in a draw. As you well know, Hex cannot end in a draw.

I posted once on rec.games.go, asking why not use the pie rule
instead of komi. The answer was, the pie rule is too crude for Go.
Any initial move along an edge should not be swapped, and any other
move should (with one possible exception, which I forget.) Komi, by
the way, is essentially a scoring handicap given to the 2nd player,
to assure both players are statistically equally likely to win.

So, if you are asking me why Hex would be so different, my answer is
that it is not fundamentally different in this respect. On a small
11x11 grid, the game is much shallower than chess, or Go on the same
size grid. So naturally, the first move advantage would be more
significant. But this is not an inherent attribute of Hex per se.
