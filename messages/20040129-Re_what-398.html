Abstract games are in a sense games without a strong theme or
setting.  Examples of abstract games are Chess and Go.  Games
such as Monopoly, Settlers of Catan and Squad Leader are not.


Am I mistaken in thinking that a game whose mechanics could be as easily
explained without the theme is a suitable topic for this group, even if the
game *is* presented with that theme? But that if the mechanics are
complicated enough so that they are too hard to learn except as a model of
some aspect of the real world, then the game is not a suitable topic?


  Sort of, I guess.  At some level, even Monopoly is an "abstract game,"
albeit a really complicated one.  It's fuzzy around the edges.  I would
say that football is not an abstract game.  Neither is poker.  Neither
is Dungeons and Dragons.  Neither are Quake or Warcraft.  Risk is on
the cusp, IMHO: it's almost a perfect-information game, but it's got
little quirks and things, and it relies heavily on diplomacy whereas
"abstract games" rely most heavily on strategy of the sort that can be
expressed with a game tree.
  As for this newsgroup, IMHO it's /de facto/ aimed at the abstract games
that are "less popular."  You ask or post here about relatively abstract
games that don't fit into any of the other Usenet games groups (i.e., not
so much chess or card games or Tetris; but new abstract games, or obscure
ones, or questions about implementing games using the game tree idea
mentioned above, might be on-topic).
