But see, you're still making reference to individual units in most of those
examples. The fact that you don't actually name them (like VI Corps or
something) doesn't matter. Also, securing supply lines is clearly strategic.
How you go about it (where to place your garrison units) is a tactical
decision. Flanking an enemy could be either: are you talking an enemy unit
(tactics) or army (strategy)? "Attacking when you have a tactical
advantage": clearly a strategy. It can apply to any number of situations all
across a game. Just because tactics ultimately implement strategies does not
mean the strategies aren't strategies, if you follow me.

But I agree there are still problems with my definition. I think one of the
best ways to answer this question is with common sense. I mean, think about
what you would say when commenting on a certain play or style of play. Would
you say, "that's a great (or lousy) TACTIC, or STRATEGY"?? Honest Abe said
that common sense ain't so common, but I think that's at least partially
true because we tend to over-analyze things and get into a "paralysis by
analysis" mode. Simple fact is, 99% of gamers have an innate understanding
of the differences btw tactics and strategy, even if they can't articulate
them.

But this discussion could go on for days nevertheless :-)

I've always thought of strategy to be what you do without making

reference

to individual units/pieces in a game. Take chess for example: I could

say

I

have a defensive strategy, or an offensive strategy, or a strategy to
control the center of the board, etc. But as soon as I talk about

individual

pieces, like what I like to do with my queenside rook, then it's

tactics.


The same holds true for wargames. In Bitter Woods (about the Battle of

the

Bulge), I can talk about what rivers I want to hold, what towns I'll

give

up

without a fight, or where I'd like my front lines to be by turn 4.

That's

all strategy. When I start to say that such-and-such a corps needs to be

to

hex 2014 by turn 3, then that's tactics.

Take go: my strategy would be how loosely I arrange my pieces at first,

or

whether I immediately attack an opponent's corner, or something like

that.

Tactics would be how I position individual pieces (making reference to

the

intersections, for example).

It's all a question of granularity. I think the scope of the game counts

for

a lot. What might be strategic in Bitter Woods is too granular for even

a

discussion of tactics in Axis and Allies. So the game's scope is a big
determinant of what will be classed as strategy and what tactics.

Hope this helps!

CND
