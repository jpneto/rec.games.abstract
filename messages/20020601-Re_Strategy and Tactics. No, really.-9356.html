Glenn C. Rhoads <gcrhoads@yahoo.com> writes:

The fact that such a strategic looking sacrifice had never occurred
before completely justifies the statement "Computer programs are not
supposed to be able to make sacrifices for strategic reasons."


Nonsense.  There has to be a first time for anything.  Because no one
has run 1500 meters in less than 3:25, would you say, "People are not
supposed to be able to run 1500 meters in less than 3:25"?  It's silly.


As I previously mentioned, it wasn't really a sacrifice for strategic
reasons but rather a sacrifice for tactical reasons.


This is a nonexistent distinction.


Suppose you are playing a game (chess, go, whatever) against a
computer and further suppose you are not aware of any instances of a
computer program using strategy X.  Now suppose that much to your
surprise, the computer program uses strategy X.  Your reaction might
be

"Whoa!  I didn't know a computer program could do THAT!"

or equivalently,

"What?!  Computer programs aren't supposed to be able to do THAT!"


The two statements aren't equivalent.  But I wouldn't say either one.  I
know that, in fact, a computer program can do anything that a human can
do.  It's only a matter of time, whether it's happened yet or not.  I
might say, that I wasn't aware that computer programs had *yet* reached
a particular level of achievement.  But that would not at all be the
same as saying they are not "supposed" to be able to reach that level.
