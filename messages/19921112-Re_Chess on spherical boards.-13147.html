Well, there's two seperate things going on here.  One is the topological
shape of the board, and the other is the game we will play on it.

First, consider the shape of your board.  It _is_ a sphere, but the
"pinching" of the end causes _all_ of the squares in the bottom row
to touch each other at a point.  B1 shares an edge with A1, B2, and
C1; and share a corner with A2 and C2 (in the usual way) and with
D1, E1, F1, G1, and H1 (because of the pinch).  Again, the board
_is_ a sphere.

OK, now consider the rules of the game.  The rules don't handle what
to do with a square that meets 5 (!) squares at one of its corners
instead of just one, so the rules need a patch.  Fine, you just simply
declare that
    A1 meets E1 and doesn't meet C1, D1, F1, or G1
    B1 meets F1 and doesn't meet D1, E1, G1, or H1
    etc.
This is a reasonable solution, and it makes the game quite playable
(except for the diagonal problem).  The problem is that THE BOARD IS
NO LONGER A SPHERE (in fact, it isn't even continous, which is why the
diagonals are strange).  Again, this doesn't mean that the game we are
playing fun or worthwhile, it just means we're not playing it on a sphere.

This does makes sense, right?  We can't take to regions of a shere that
share a corner, alter the sphere so that they don't share a corner any
more, and still expect to have a sphere.

It happens to preserve other "nice" properties as well.  For instance,
diagonals are well defined, with one-color bishops.  Also, if you pick
any two adjacent squares for the white king and queen to start on, and
then put the other 30 pieces down in the correct squares (relative to
the king and queen), the game is mathematically the same no matter which
initial squares you picked.
