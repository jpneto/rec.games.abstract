Agreed. In any case, the difference between Go and Chess is not that between east and west, since both are of Asiatic origin. Furthermore, Chess, in one form or another, remains pretty well the national game of Asia. While both are equally Asiatic, Wei-qi (the ancestor of Japanese Go) is more specifically Chinese, so the contrast is between China and the rest of Asia rather than between Asia and the west. Indeed, until about the middle of the 19th century "The West" has hardly come up with any original game of its own.

In the case of both Chess and Go, some individual was probably messing
around with some equipment which was used for some purpose other than
game playing, and discovered the simple mechanism and principle that
would form the basis of the game.

I should think that this, in one form or another, accounts for the origin of nearly all games.

 Something resembling a Go board and
Go stones may have been used for divination.

Possibly. Stewart Culin, the 19th century ethnographer and games researcher, made great play of the divinatory origin of board and table games, but I think he went a bit overboard on it. It certainly seems a likely explanation for Wei-qi, as it is supported by documentary evidence, but there are many other possible source activities for other games. Think, for example, of accounting and book-keeping devices, and surely Mancala owes something to seed or bean planting?

What I'm getting around to is that a Chess-like game and a Go-like
game were designed by two different individuals, people found them
entertaining, and the games evolved, probably Chess more so than Go,
while retaining their essential identities.

It may be misleading to think of the origins of traditional board games in terms of individuals. It is natural for us to do so because we live in an age that has become extremely conscious of the significance and importance of individuality. Folk games, like folk tales and folk dances, are perhaps better regarded as the products of cultures and communities rather than of individuals.
