and Jan Kristian Haugland replied:

... It seems that we get the P-positions by starting with P_0 = {0, 0} and
adding recursively for k >= 1

P_k = {smallest positive integer not used, same + k}

This gives: {1, 2}, {3, 5}, {4, 7}, {6, 10}, {8, 13}, {9, 15},
{11, 18}, {12, 20}, {14, 23}, {16, 26}, {17, 28}, ...

I guess it's tricky to find a closed form expression for these.


to which David desJardins remarked:

I think these are the pairs {floor(k*phi), floor(k*phi^2)} for
k=1,2,3,..., where phi=(1+sqrt(5))/2.  These have the well-known
property that the k-th pair has gap size k, and every natural number
occurs exactly once in the sequence.


By pure twist of fate, I happened upon this game in "Exploring Mathematics
With Your Computer", by Arthur Engel, page 169.  It is called "Wythoff's
Game".  An exercise on page 171 confirms David desJardins statement, and
suggests it can be proven using "Beatty's Theorem" about complementary
sequences.
