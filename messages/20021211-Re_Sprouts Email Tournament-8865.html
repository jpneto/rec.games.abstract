That's a great summary of the old view of sprouts.  I remember many
years ago at a chess tournament the math professor David Sumner
expressed exactly the same idea.  Lately, though, the concept of
"survivors" has given the game a strong strategical element.  A
survivor is a live spot in an exhausted position.

I think you really have two ideas, one of which is very valid.  It is
true, or seems to be from my experience, that advantages do not
accumulate in sprouts the way they do in chess or go.  If that feature
is necessary for your interest, sprouts is probably not for you.  On
the other hand, I also know from my own experience that there are
strategical ideas that can give us guidance and orientation in the
absence of a total calculation.  The survivor concept is the best
example of such a strategical idea.  A given position might have just
enough liberties to make 3 or 4 survivors the likely outcome.  One
player will need 3, the other 4.  The first player will try to "dry
up" the position while the second player will play for fecundity.  The
players can meaningfully make these attempts even if they cannot do
the complete calculation.  For instance, enclosing moves tend to be
more fecund.


A live spot is either a "cannibal" or a "ghost".  A cannibal is a live
spot with 0 or two lines attached.  A ghost is a live spot with one
line attached.
Any sprouts move must change a position with an even number of
cannibals to a position with an odd number of cannibals, and vice
versa.  Since a survivor is a cannibal, the player to move in any
position can count the number of cannibals and instantly know whether
he needs an even or an odd number of survivors in the final position.


Using nim notation, a cannibal is equal to 0, a ghost is equal to *. The 
number of cannibals in a position is irrelevant, as each cannibal 
provides either no move, or a move for each player.  If you are going to 
move to a position with nothing but cannibals and ghosts, and you can 
arrange for the number of ghosts to be even, you win by doing so;  and 
if you make it odd, you lose by doing so.

Sometimes there
will be cases in which a player succeeds too well, minimizes or

intuition in estimating and trying to influence how many survivors a
position or subposition will yield.

A surprisingly useful notion is the concept of a "universal trap" or
UT.  A UT is a subposition consisting of two communicating spots, each
with 0 lines attached, and such that either (1) neither spot
communicates with any other spot or (2) both spots communicate with
one other spot, a spot with two lines attached and which itself
communicates with at least one more spot.  We generally try not to
move to a UT because the second person to move to a UT can determine
whether the UT will yield an even or an odd number of survivors.


Do you mean "we generally try not to move _in_ a UT"?  This would make 
sense, as the only move in a UT is to *2.


UT (1) has the value {*2|*2}, which is equal to 0.  This means that, 
with competent play, it might as well not exist.  But if you can't 
evaluate the position and you think that your opponent may be able to, 
it is a good idea to avoid moving in a UT (1), as it increases your 
opponent's options.  (It also increases your own options, but this is no 
use to you while you can't evaluate the position.)


There are two types of UTs, as I elliptically indicated.  You're
missing the fact that a move into the second type of UT can be
advantageous by affecting the UT itself and the configuration beyond
the UT.  But, I think you're getting the main idea.  The strategical
creation of a UT is often an attempt to simplify advantageously.  But
the creation of UTs also seems to be an efficient way to produce more
survivors.

Again, you can always evaluate the entire position, imperfectly but
usefully, by applying strategic ideas.




These ideas and some related ideas explained on the website
www.geocities.com/chessdp help make the game a lot of fun to play from

Would you say the same about nim?


I would say nim suffers from too much strategy.  There is a grand
strategy, the Sprague-Grundy technique, that allows us to evaluate any
position,easily and infallibly, without forming a tree of analysis at
all.




In the kind of position which you describe in which a player knows he
is lost, it's the same as in chess.  If the player knows his opponent
has the technique and knowledge to certainly win, he resigns.
Otherwise he tries to make problems for his opponent, using strategy
and tactics.  But most of sprouts play occurs in difficult, unclear
positions.  One player or the other invariably resigns long before the
position is exhausted.  Beginners are often amazed how early
resignation comes.  In fact, resignation usually comes long before
meaningful, nonrandom play would have even begun in the old days
before the survivor concept.
