 Xong[tm] is an abstract game for 2 players or, in special arrangements, more
 players.  it is a territory game.  Xong was invented in 1996 - seven years
 ago.  It is a mutant variant of an earlier invention of mine, QuintHex[tm].
 QuintHex is played on an order-4 hexagonal grid of hexagons.  the pieces are
 made of the sides of the connected hexagons and the objective is to rope off
 hexagons with legs of your own color so that your opponent cannot place a
 piece in a way to make a leg touch your hexagons.  there are 12 quintastix,
 pieces made of 5 joined legs.  each player has a complete set of quintastix
 in their own color.  you could box out areas of pieces the other player had
 already played & get ahead.  QuintHex suffered from one of the most common
 ailments of abstract games - the first player has an advantage.  Xong is a
 real candidate solution to this problem, as far as i can see.  note: to be
 completely above board, i must note that i have unvieled this game right here
 in rec.games.abstract years ago under a different name, Zoids.  however, a
 search on Zoids produced a dinosaur model playset for children - completely
 different, but too close for comfort.  i was trying to find out what the
 australian aborigine word for those dried-up lakes that make a tesselated
 caked mud surface and a friend was suggesting "billabong", which was a dried-
 up oxbow kind of thing and, POW just like that, the sport clothing line came
 out!  :-D  - so i have renamed my game Xong.  Xong is also a vietnamese word
 meaning "end of an exam" when i google it, along with various proper names on
 other hits.

 currently, the world's best abstract game [IMO] is still Go and will probably
 remain so for the forseeable human future.  Chess, counting the eastern world
 varieties as well as the western world's version, continues to be a very fine
 game.  these two games suffer from the first move advantage and, in the case
 of Go, there are still lengthy ongoing efforts to determine a komi to offset
 this advantage perfectly.  there are also some poorly worded versions of the
 Rules for Go that have led to misunderstandings, especially in the western
 hemisphere. Chess has undergone many revisions of it's Rules and, in the case
 of western chess, has retained some peculiar features, like En Passant.
 Chess suffers from being rather drawish and infact has developed a small
 battery of tools to bring to bear when it reaches an impasse or repetitive
 gridlock situation.  Go has a marvelous defect in it's crystalline Ruleset
 regarding the Ko.  this defect is truly marvelous, lending a vast rich wealth
 of interesting play to the action.  but it arises naturally out of the board
 & stone patterns.

 there are many other noble abstract games out there.  r.g.a. knows them.
 some of them have been invented by contributors to this group, infact.

 Xong is by no means an attempt to challenge these games.  whereas Go has
 been compared to an entire campaign of war and Chess to perhaps one of it's
 battles, Xong might then be analogous to a swordfight on the battlefield.
 typically Go games take hundreds of moves to determine a victor while chess
 may take 80 or so moves.  Xong takes around 31 moves.  i am not sure what
 this may have to do with the game's complexity.  i find the complexity quite
 enough to have kept me up long hours of the night working out variations.

 Xong is a position game, but there is no capturing.  Xong never gets into a
 repetitive impasse or deadlock.  however, Xong also has a defect in it's
 Ruleset:  at the end of play, when one player is unable to give a new Xoid
 to the other, instead, as a means of compensation, he gives 1 point.  this
 prevents ties, but is philosophically an artificial construct outside of the
 geometry of the board & piece patterns.  this is called the Xong point.

 you may have noticed i referred to giving new pieces.  this is central to the
 operation of the game.  it is like baseball in that you give a piece to the
 other player to play.  then after it is played, you are given back a new
 piece to play.  it's like hitting a pitch.  naturally you want to give your
 opponent the piece that does the least damage to you.  the pieces, called
 xoids, begin with the smallest ones and gradually increase in size and
 complexity until there is no more room on the board for any new piece.  each
 piece can only appear once in a game.  since secured hexagons are worth 2
 points, at the end of the game one player will have an even score and the
 other will have an odd score because of the Xong point.

 there are currently 2 active versions of Xong - a Standard game of 61
 hexagons arranged in an order-5 hexagonal grid, and a Random variant that
 generates random boards of about 57-68 hexagons arranged in one blobby weird
 shape that is never the same.  the random version might seem to violate the
 letter of law regarding abstract games by introducing an element of chance,
 but once the board is created, both players are in possession of 100% of the
 game information.  so nothing is hidden.  the Random variation was created to
 head off any stockpiling of memorized openings, such as has happened in Go
 and Chess.  -->Standard Xong already has an Audrey Gambit Declined opening.

 i have a website with some java applets (j2sdk1.4.0_01 applets) for single
 workstation play.  there are new-window browser links to the 7 rules, a full
 example Standard game replayable with a java applet, a table of some of the
 pieces, a Standard game playing java applet and a Random game playing java
 applet.  these playing applets automatically keep a running score & shade in
 hexagons for each side, greening out hexes touched by both colors & cashing
 hexes* that are roped off as they occur during play.  i do not have a way to
 play against an opponent live over the net like the go & chess servers do.  i
 am working on this and hope to unveil the world's first Internet Xong Server
 someday soon.  also, someday i will implement double buffered imaging to make
 dragging xoids around on the board not so blinkety-blinkety all the time.
