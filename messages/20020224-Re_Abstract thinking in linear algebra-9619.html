Let me give you a concrete instance.



Suppose a company decided one day to take all their best and
brightest employees and have them do a stint on the factor
floor in assembly.



Here's one of the processes.  You're to remove a box from a
carrying platform, and open it up.  One by one, take an item

>from inside the box (let's say there's 6), open it up, remove

its contents -- consisting of a unit, power supply other stuff
and the packing materials.  Place the unit on the testing
conveyor, place the power supply on a crate and put the
other stuff in a 3rd place and the emptied box at the end of
the line so that the next person can use it to recrate the stuff.



Now ... these so-called bright people are being asked to do a
couple days of this.  All you hear is the grumbling ("I didn't
go to [sic] college (actually trade school) to learn how to
program so I could end up doing this", or "how is this going
to help me?") and various other gripes you typically get like
when a spoiled Hollywood is asked to do some restaurant work in
the kitchen.



But the REALLY bright one proceeded to do this:



 (1) Grab ALL the boxes off the carrying platform (let's say 6 of them)
 (2) Simultaneously turn them ALL upside down, and let the
     contents slide out
 (3) Place all the boxes on the other end
 (4) Line up all 36 of the items dumped out and in rapid-fire sequence
     cut them all open (floor space required)
 (5) Sort the items into heaps
 (6) Take the 36 items in the unit heap and place them all on the
     conveyor
 (7) Take the 36 items in the power supply heap and place them all
     on the power supply crate
 (8) Place the 36 items of the 3rd heap on the 3rd crate
 (9) Offer assistance to the people down the line or otherwise take
     a break while they finish up with your stuff -- because you ended
     up averaging 3 TIMES FASTER than the norm!



And no, it's not multidimensional.  This is the SAME brightness that
made this person so damn good at their work where, in fact, their
performance is showing the very same 3-fold, 4-fold, etc. disparity;
as well as in nearly every other task he performs.


But what if you put the person on an assembly line, with
the job to tighten a bolt for every item coming down the
line.  Or even the one you stated where you are not allowed
to take more than one, or it is too heavy to turn upside
down?  Doing it for a day or so might be tolerable, but
day after day is not going to get good results.

If you are on an assembly line, you cannot average faster
than the norm, as you have no control over the rate.

During WWII, patriotic American college students
volunteered for assembly lines and piecework.  On assembly
lines, they lasted for a while, and then just became
totally sloppy.  The ones doing piecework were like
the bright one, doing much better than the norm, until
the boredom set in.  The only way out of it is to at least
temporarily destroy that part of the brain.
