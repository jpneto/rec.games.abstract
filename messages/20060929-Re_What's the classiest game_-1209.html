Quoting patrick55carroll@yahoo.com's message of Wednesday:

Is it just me, or does it seem that abstract, perfect-information,
deterministic (no randomity) strategy games are generally considered
the classiest of all games?

In some environments I am disappointed to see how much they are sidelined, even derided.  Some of this could be inverse snobbery, of course.

Contrast that with backgammon, another age-old classic.  Popular
opinion seems to hold that it's a lesser game--probably because of the
dice.  Even bridge, though highly regarded, somehow lacks the class
that chess and go enjoy.

My perception of backgammon is that it is regarded as a classy game in some circles.  My perception is more to do with who has played these games, though.  Chess and go have grandmasters, and chess has noted royal players.  Backgammon was popular among 18th century clergy, and in the 19th century it was played often by Gladstone.  I don't see the same associations with Settlers of Catan, Acquire, or Lines of Action.

Maybe I'm the only one who sees this phenomenon.  Am I just imagining things, or do some kinds of games have a classier reputation than others?

It's very subjective, though, isn't it.  I don't see things like Gipf, Hex and Dvonn in the same light.  My criteria are different to yours, though in recognising a game as "classy" I don't necessarily recognise it as good.  In recognising the game as good, I don't necessarily want to play it, but that's another matter altogether.

And while we're at it, which game do you think is the classiest--and
why?

Chess has to be the classiest game for me, though it's the one I least want to play.  It generated so much literature, attracted so many grandmasters, and players such as Caliph al-Mamun and Queen Elizabeth I, that I can't help but recognise that it must have good reason for such universal respect.

But all this is subjective and arbitrary: ask again tomorrow and you might get a different answer from me.
