Quoting marksteere@gmail.com's message of Friday:

Some mechanisms in new games appear contrived to me.  I can't think of
an example here and now, but at least one of Sid Sackson's games struck
me this way.

Contrivance is a good thing.  No abstract game, including Checkers,
would exist without it.

Maybe "too contrived" would have been a better phrase, less apt to misinterpretation.  Or even "too abstract" (!).  If a game can't have simple mechanisms for one reason or another, I at least like to see some "metaphor or sytlistic representation", as Patrick put it.

Being able to move a piece by a distance equal to the number of pieces on its row, column or diagonal is the sort of mechanism that turns me off.  Some people love this sort of game.  Where my sentiments differ from those in your own earlier post, is that I relish the fact that while some designers experiement with novel mechanisms, others continue to find new ways to use the existing ones.

The only game in Sid's book "Gamut of Games" that I found mildly
interesting was Lines of Action, a game often attributed to Sid but
which was actually invented by Claude Soucie.

This might be one of the games that turned me off, but it's that long since I've seen Sackson's book that I can't remember.
