Yes.  That was the idea back in 1998, back in the dark days after
Kasparov's loss to Deep Blue.  I had the notion that it would be an
interesting challenge to devise a game that was simpler than chess
(not a hard criterion to satisfy) yet more resistant to computer
analysis.  My idea was that a game in which players constructed their
own pieces and continually chose whether to improve or move them might
do the trick.  The jury, of course, is still out.


I also was inspired by the G.K. - D.B. match to see if there could
be a game that would be difficult for computers to play and still be
easy for humans. See http://arimaa.com/arimaa/ for more info.

Unlike OCTI I did not start from scratch and limited myself to find 
such a game using only the material available in a standard chess set. 
Thus people can play it without needing to buy anything. It took me 
much longer to finalize the rules of Arimaa than it did for you to 
finalize the rules of OCTI. Probably since I had more constraints :-)
See http://arimaa.com/arimaa/about/ for more info.
