There are three "families" of chess.  Represented by examples, they are:

 o  Western Chess 
 o  Shogi
 o  Xianqi 

Each family has several examples.  "Korean Chess" is an example of the
Xianqi type.  Each game in the family shares certain characteristics with
the others (general look of the board, types of pieces, etc.)

What they generally have in common is a king, a rook type piece, a
knight, pawns that promote, a piece that moves diagnally (the guards
and elephants of Xianqi, the bishop and generals of Shogi, the queen
and bishops of Western Chess) either one space or many.  The diagnal
moving pieces mutated the most.  The rooks mutated into lances in
Shogi (I've heard the Shogi bishop and rook were recent Western Chess
add-ons to Shogi).  The guards/ elephants/generals mutated into the
bishop and queen in Western Chess.  The knight stayed pretty constant
although specific rules vary (the knight is limited in Shogi to
forward moves and can jump pieces in Western Chess).  Then there is
the cannon.

As an aside, a friend an I once created "Fuzzy Chess" using all 3
types of pieces on a Shogi board.  It looked pretty neat.  The two
stage pawns (they split -- stack a Western Chess pawn on a Chinese
Chess pawn) were kind of interesting. :-)
