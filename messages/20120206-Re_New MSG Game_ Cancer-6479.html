So what??  In Oust, also a game of annihilation, I've seen one player
get down to one stone against a hundred enemy stones, an
"overwhelming" material disadvantage, and then turn the game around
and win.  The effect is even more extreme in Rive, a territory game
like Go.  You can be completely wiped out to zero stones more than
once and *still* come back and win.


the parity curse.


Yep.  If it's down to two groups, one of each player's color, with a
few unoccupied points to fill in, then yes, it's boiled down to a game
of Nim at that point.

I don't like that aspect of Cancer, but by the same token, parity is
only a "curse" if you can't handle parity.  What do you think Dots and
Boxes is?   A big game of parity.


I'd be glad to be proved wrong, but if these endgame
situations with capricious outcomes are a common occurrence
as I suspect, deriving any serious strategical planning
for the earlier stages of the game might be unapproachable.


I suspect capricious Cancer outcomes are more likely on a toy, 5x5x5
board.  How much serious strategery were you hoping to derive from toy
Cancer?  How strategic would toy Go be?

If the Nimliness of Cancer is really a problem, maybe we *should*
consider non-cancerous eyes.  Completely different game though.  Now
both players will not always have a move available.
