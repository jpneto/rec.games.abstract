Quoting Mitoshima's message of Sunday:

I have always looked at discovering new game ideas, however, in other to make something good and solid, if you know what I mean, I have to start with the fundamentals first and the reason why I chose the most popular Tic Tac Toe. Once I have mastered these fundamentals, I can then look further over the horizon, but for now, I'm keeping it low.

At one time I wondered why so many people released their own versions of tic tac toe into the world (or noughts & crosses as I usually call it). It was some time later, as a budding writer of computer games, with a catalogue of too-ambitious half-finished projects, that I figured out why.

You do right to start simple, though there is, to be honest, a limit to how simple one can go without writing something that nobody will play. Might I make a suggestion?  With your modifications to tic tac toe, you're inventing a game as well as trying to code its AI.  If you look at a book like Murray's History of Board-Games Other than Chess, or Bell's Board and Table Games from Many Civilizations, you'll find a great variety of very simple games that are much more playable than tic tac toe, whose strategies are already known, and which may generate a bit more interest.  Three men's morris, four field kono, and mu-torere are examples I can think of without getting the books out.  You'll find information about these on the internet too.

But If you've put a lot of work into your tic tac toe game, then you'll probably want to see it through.  In that case, good luck to you, I hope you have fun with it and that it leads you to more interesting projects.
