No offense intended, Bill, but it should be clear from the diagram
that the closed end is simply a hexagonally tessellated, regular
hexagon.  I know some people have had trouble with the diagram so,
just for their benefit, let me describe how the cylinder is
constructed.  You cut the star shape from the rule sheet, not
including the wide arcs, so that you have a regular hexagon with a
long rectangle extending outward from each of the six sides, a
starfish shape.  Then you make six 90 degree folds where the arms meet
the body.  Once the cutout has been so folded, the rectangles are
joined side by side and you have a roughly cylindrical shape with a
hexagonal cross section.  One end is open and the other end is closed.

If you take the Super Lariat board (http://www.marksteeregames.com/
Super_Lariat_rules.html) and cut off one of the three protrusions, you
have the cylinder used in Lariat.  If you look at the fourth picture
in the Super Lariat rules, you can see that the center cell on the end
is highlighted.

The whole point of applying the lariat principle to a cylinder was to
make the end, center cell no more valuable than any other cell -
certainly not "key".  For the sake of argument, say the length of the
cylinder is 10 times the circumference.  It'd be a lot easier to
circle the cylinder, to form a lariat near the lip, than to extend a
ray all the way from the end, center cell to the lip.  The end, center
cell would have much less strategic value than a lip cell for
example.  For ergonomics, I chose not to use an exceedingly long
cylinder - just long enough to considerably devalue the end, center
cell.

I have to strongly disagree with you there, Bill.  Lariat isn't even
distantly related to Hex beyond the fact that they're both connection
games played on a hexagonally tessellated board, even with the skirted
umbrella incarnation.  In Hex, Black has an assigned orientation 90
degrees perpendicular to White's orientation.  Technically it's an
unequal goals game, even though the goals are the same but just
rotated 90 degrees.  Lariat is an equal goals game with no preassigned
orientations for the players.  Even if you choose to claim the end,
center cell, you're not committed to drawing a ray from there to the
lip.
