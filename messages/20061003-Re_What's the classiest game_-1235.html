  No, that's not what he said. I got it backwards at first, too, but then
I stopped to think about his example. He said, in effect:

    You can simulate a die roll with simultaneous movement.

The converse is definitely /not/ true; you can't simulate simultaneous
movement with a die roll.

  This theorem was I think introduced in this thread to counter the idea (a straw man nobody's really arguing, IMHO) that, say, Snakes & Ladders
could be /superior/ to a game with no explicit randomness but with
simultaneous movement; because you can rewrite the rules of Snakes &
Ladders, using simultaneous movement to determine each die roll, and
you'll get an equivalent game with no explicit randomness.

  To put it another way: A die roll can be simulated by a zero-sum game
where your optimum strategy is to move randomly; and vice versa. However,
there /are/ zero-sum games with /different/ strategies, and these games
/cannot/ be simulated by die rolls.
