What I meant, was a computer program, which resolutes the game. Be it in the
form of a PBM-game (that's where I come from game-wise) or a regular computer
game. The procedural part is that when a player announces he wishes to do this
or that, the resolution of this plan is utterly procedural.


Game playing programs seem to work better when rule-driven.
Now there may be a difference between the rules of the game and the
game itself, or the play of the game.  How is that you see current
games as procedural, and what would a declarative game be in contrast?

What is procedural? Well, IMHO, today's games (excepting role-playing games
for the moment) are designed such that the 'body' or 'heart' of the game
(call it semantics if you will) is a procedural definition of what happens
when I do this. The computer (be he a machine or a human, I mean someone who
computes the resolution to a given game situation) is the 'heart' of the game.
It is not the data (the current state of the game world) that determines by
itself what happens, it is the computer, who looks at the data and then does
something with it. I hope I am not too obscure...

Also, I may be completely wrong on this distinction, because way down on the
lowest level of looking at what happens, EVERYTHING is procedural. Even some
declarative specification is computed/resoluted one step at a time. Does
anyone, except me, see the difference, however, between this low-level look,
which is completely uninteresting for the player, and the high-level look,
where everything is declarative?

Well, just an afterthought: In a procedural game rules may be declarative,but
computation is not. In a declarative game both rules and resolution will be
declarative. Furthermore, in a declarative game resolution will not only be
dependent on the game-situation as in procedural games, but it will actually
be DRIVEN by the game-situation.
