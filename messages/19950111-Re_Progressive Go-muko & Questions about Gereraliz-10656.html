6. 4 in a row, 4 direstions (NSEW). A draw can be forced by the
   second player. To do this, each square has a well defined

Well I don't know about the rest of the Sanderson article, but this makes it
look a bit dodgy!

4-direction 4-in-a-row is an easy win for starter. (I suspect Sanderson may
have been referring to the complementary-squares proof that 4-direction
5-in-a-row is a draw; which is indeed fairly easy.)

4-direction 4-in-a-row is easily won in 15 half-moves or less as follows.
Starter need only take care that he doesn't force seconder to make an open 2.

. . 4 . .
. 2 1 . .
6 5 3 9 A
. . 7 B .
. . 8 . .

giving position

. . O . .
. O X . .
O X X X O
. . X X .
. . O . .     and a win on move 15.

There are alternatives for white, but none make a significant difference.
