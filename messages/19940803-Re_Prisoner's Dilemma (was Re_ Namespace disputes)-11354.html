Under certain circumstances, you can beat tit-for-tat.  The 
not-quite-trivial such circumstance is a tournament with a finite 
number of unchanging competitors.  If you introduce only one new 
competitor, and it can have arbitrary complexity, it can be 
fine-tuned to use a different strategy against each separate opponent. 


   In a Nature article a year or so ago, someone did an evolutionary study
of the prisoner's dilemma to see which strategies would come out on top.
In some of the cases, forgiving tit-for-tat emerged, but in more of the
cases, a strategy they called win-stay, lose-switch dominated.  Basically,
if you "win" (you defect-opp coops, you coop-opp coops) you do whatever
you did last turn.  If you "lose" (you coop-opp defects, you defect-opp
defects) you do the opposite of whatever you did last turn.  I don't have
a reference, but this study looked pretty definitive to me.
