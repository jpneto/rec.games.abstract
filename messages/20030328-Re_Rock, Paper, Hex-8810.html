I don't think that's a good idea. Whoever lost the bidding would have to
also get to place a hex. If they got to choose with knowledge of where the
other player already went, it could actually be an advantage to lose the
bid. Not that that automatically means the system's broken, but I think it
makes it more complex without making it richer.



dvdavins@earthlink.net wrote:

Yesterday I thought up a new hex variant, that I thought might be of
interest, even though it isn't quite a game of perfect information.
Actually, I doubt I'm the first to come up with this idea wrt Hex, and

it

could be (and sometimes has been) applied to many abstract games.

The idea is to have simultaneous movement, rather than the swap rule.

Each

player secretly indicated their intended hex, and then the intentions

are

revealed. If each player chooses the same hex, then the process is

repeated

except that the originally intended hex of off-limits until the next

time

pieces are successfully placed (sort of like in Diplomacy).

One thing to note, is that while the concept is somewhat applicable to
almost any game, in most of them (I suspect) it would lead to frequent
simultaneous wins for each side. (Imagine checkmate being delivered to

each

king in chess.) In Hex that can't happen, though it could lead to draws

if

all remaining hexes have placed off-limits so that no moves are

possible.


I'm not a very good Hex player, so I don't think I should be the one to

test

this. But I'd be happy to referee, receiving the secret moves and

reporting

them when I've received them from both players. I suggest an even-hexed
board, so as to reduce the chance of first-turn deadlocks. If anyone is
interested in playing, please reply on- or off-line.
