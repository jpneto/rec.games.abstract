In article <ByLEFC.HJJ@hermes.hrz.uni-bielefeld.de>, marc@techfak.uni-bielefeld.de (Marc Rehmsmeier) writes:
|> Equalization in chess doesn't seem to be a good idea.
|> It does not avoid the necessary of learning analysed
|> openings. Quite the reverse. Consider the following
|> situation:
|> 
|> Player A is a specialist in a certain opening.
|> Player B feels very uncomfortable playing this opening.
|> With his first two white and one black moves he can
|> force the situation on the board to a preferred
|> structure. Now he is able to play the game with both
|> white and black very well (because he has learned the
|> analysed theory of this opening). But player B hates
|> this opening, no matter whether he elects black or white.
|> So Player A has got a big advantage.
|> 
|> Marc Rehmsmeier.

Quite on the contrary, I think, this could be a good form of chess
since players would have to have a wider (and hence shallower!!)
opening reportoire.  This would avoid the 20-30-first-moves-from-
theory tendencies sometimes seen in chess today, and would
create more exciting situations quicker...

Of course, knowledge of opening theory would still be a big
advantage, but general understanding of opening principles and
versatility would become comparatively more important.

(Although deep down I prefer the game as it is... :-))
