What this set of game rules adds to chess - 
1) Activators, pieces that allow other pieces to perform actions which they could not without the presence of activators.
2) Multiple moves per turn. In Macysburg, for example, about midgame, there can be around 100 pieces on the board, all of which may be capable of moving each turn.
3) Differential squares. In modern chess there are white squares and black squares. Macysburg uses 4 colors of squares, white, brown, green, and gray. Just as bishops are excluded from the wrong color squares, the brown and green squares exclude different types of pieces. The gray squares are generally goal squares, and affect winning conditions. 
4) Different set-ups for each individual scenario. The 3 colored squares, green, brown, and gray, are individually placed on the board for each game, allowing a different board for each play of a specific scenario. The board itself is variable in size. 
5) Variable winning conditions. The games use multiple activators which take the place of the kings. Generally it is easier to win by achieving geographic objectives rather than eliminating so many of the opponent's activators that the opposing army is unable to move or to move effectively.
6) Replacements and reinforcements coming on board at various times during a game.

What this set of game rules removes from chess -
1) All "infinite sliders". Pieces move 1, 2, or 3 squares at most.
2) Castling is unnecessary, as pieces start more deployed than in standard forms of chess.
3) Checkmate, as there are multiple "kings", the activators, which can be captured during the game.
4) Promotion. Promotion is the attaining of a geographic objective - the other side of the board - and goal squares, like promotion valuable for victory, replace the need for promotion.

The game is a hybrid of chess and wargames, a simulation of a wargame running on a chess engine. You can see rules, example set-ups, and some discussion by following the links found here: http://anotherlevel-games.com/home/command_and_maneuver/
