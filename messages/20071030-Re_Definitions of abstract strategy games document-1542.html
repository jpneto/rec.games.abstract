Now I've learned that there is some degree of consensus that abstract
games may be "lightly themed."  People who play lightly themed games
will tell you that the light theme has absolutely no bearing on the
gameplay and that such games are "essentially" abstract.  Still, I
have some difficulty letting go of my view of abstract games being
themeless, Reversi, Go, and Checkers being good examples.


A game like "Through the Desert" for example, is lightly themed.  By

a real abstract is can be concretely determined without having to
mention theme.

I'll have to let Rich answer this for himself, but I would like to
second the question.  (Sorry Rich, but it's an obvious question and
one that will be asked again and again, Pete's being only the first
public instance.)  I can say that the AG document in question relates
to the new group IAGO (International Abstract Games Organization
[abstractgamers.org]), founded by Rich.  When IAGO was first proposed
as a "governing body" for abstract games I kind of bristled at that.
I've designed 13 (unthemed) abstract games over the past 15 years and
somehow in all that time I never had any desire to be "governed".


IAGO is meant to represent and market abstract strategy games that
don't have any sort of association covering them.  The idea came out
of when I wanted to approach some organization like IAGO to promote my
designs for Stonehenge and then found such an organization doesn't
exist.  And "governing body" is meant to be a means by which to get
standards in place with such things as the name of chess variant
pieces and so on.  Organizations can govern things a lot of ways.  I
expect governing by concensus, the way Linux is run, and any open
standards organization would.


Standardizing and governing make sense for something like a Texas Hold
'em tournament in which you want everyone playing the exact same game,
or in which specific, allowable variations are enumerated. Reasons for
standardizing abstract games as a whole have as yet eluded me.


Holding tournaments is part of it.  The second part is to have a
sufficient robust set of definitions, so that what is covered doesn't
grow so large it ends up being some sort of Table Games association,
that ends up including the likes of Role-Playing games.  You end up
creating a slipperly slope if you don't get definitions out there.
Like, I had stated there should be backgammon considered, to which
someone then said Scrabble and Carcassonne should now be part of the
association.
