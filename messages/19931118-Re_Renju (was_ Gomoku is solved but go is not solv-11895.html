From your description, it may be closer in difficulty to Checkers than
Chess.  In the Checkers program Chinook, the use of huge endgame databases
has a tremendous influence on good play.  Similarly, in Renju, a slight
modification of Allis' search technique could make finding a finishing
combination, if it exists, relatively easy for a computer program.

If the moves in the early phases of Renju are as complicated as you say,
then yes it would be extremely hard to solve, since we'd be dealing with
both a large search space and a high decision complexity.  In contrast,
Checkers has a narrow (effective) branching factor, so alpha-beta search
does well.  But I don't think the difficulty of Renju is clear yet, from a
computing standpoint.  Certainly, the strategic goal of Renju is not as
amorphous as it is in Go, or even Chess.  Perhaps time will tell.

often *disproved*, so the distinction between fact and belief is important.
