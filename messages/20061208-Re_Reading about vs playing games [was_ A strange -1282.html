Yes, but not by me.  :)  An extreme case is Nine Men's Morris, which
has been "solved"--meaning someone managed to program a computer to
play a perfect game.  Now everybody knows how it comes out if both
sides are played optimally.  But unlike computers, people don't make
perfect moves all the time.  So, the outcome is still uncertain between
two human players, and the game can still be enjoyed.  And while
they're having fun playing, two human players (or even just one playing
against a computer) can also work on improving.

To me, that's what game playing is all about--fun and improvement.
Sometimes one has to take a back seat to the other, but both are
important.

This is the important point that clarified it for me.  As a 1000 player
with your drive, I say go for it.  As you've said, there's lots of
literature, lots of fun and even exciting games to study, and lots of
clubs where you can find players at similar levels.  (I know you prefer
computers, but nothing quite beats face-2-face club play.)  The early
learning process in Chess is what I enjoyed most.  There's lots of
standard openings to learn (memorize but you really need to understand
them too), the endgame (though 100% "known" still need to be known by
you), and the middle game provides lots of opportunities to improve
your calculation and look-ahead skills.  There is still lots of "fun"
in it.  My bubble burst after I reached a certain plateau and realized
that to break the 2000 barrier I would have to begin dedicating more
and more time to activities that I determined were less and less fun.
That's when I discovered Bridge (but that's a different story).

Well, yes and no.  In truth, I may never play a game of chess or

You may well find that club-play may be essential in making some real
improvements.  I'm not a Chess pedagogue, but human players do not make
the same mistakes that a dumbed-down AI does.  Players of similar
levels make errors you can usually understand.  You can learn a great
deal from fellow players.  That step alone could well be what it would
take to raise your rating a few hundred points.  (There are lots of
online clubs but I have avoided them in the past because lots of the
players use computers to play their games and I just want to play with
a real person.  Face-2-face is much more socially satisfying anyway.)

Whether you choose Chess or Checkers, you are in for some study and
practice.  Either game I think will provide you with the challenge
you're looking for.  Despite my opinion that Checkers is "the better
game" (by which I mean just what you said, cleaner and more elegant) I
still get the most enjoyment out of a nice game of Chess.  Best of luck
to you!
