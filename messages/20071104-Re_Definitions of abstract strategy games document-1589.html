Andy Leighton wrote:

Also I don't even see the need for a board (as suc)

You don't need a board for a game to be an abstract, and of coure there are plenty of card games demonstrate that quite well. However, I think there's a point in following pragmatism as well as notions of "purity", and card games don't need a new special association for them that lumps them in with GIPF and go, as they do quite nicely without one.

But if you create an abstract card game that uses its own special deck rather than a traditional deck I can't see it being readily adopted by the card game community (says he, who isn't a part of that community, so it's a sketchily educated guess).

ISTM that a higher level of definition may be useful, and would get around this sort of problem.  Although Herman Hesse's fictional Glass Bead Game doesn't exist, /if it did/ then it strikes me as being the ultimate abstract strategy game.  But we'd have to rule it out here becuase there's no board and no pieces.  So perhaps start the definition off with key elements such as "a contest" and the "outcome determined by the mental skill of the participnats" or the likes of that.  That strikes me as far more fundamental, and probably more useful, than saying "we need a board and some bits".
