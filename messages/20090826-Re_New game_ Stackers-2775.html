In fact, you can argue that the possibility of draws is an advantage for
deterministic games, as absense of draws implies that one player has an

That's ridiculous.  I'm so tired of these crank-theoretical, "perfect
play" arguments.  One player will have an advantage anyway, regardless
of the presence or absence of draws.  Chess has a huge first move
advantage.  Its potential for draws does nothing to mitigate that.  It
only makes the game worse.  The potential for draws can't possibly be
considered an "advantage" of any sort, in real terms or in theoretical
terms.  There's going to be move order advantage no matter what.  The
degree of move order advantage depends skill level of course.  A
balance should be struck between game length and move order advantage,
by, for example, varying the board size of a scalable game, experts
getting a larger board.  Move order advantage should be maintained at
such a level that the initial coin toss is more of an annoyance than a
liability.  The coin toss will never be a total non-issue.  The
abstract game is in the end, a game of luck, no less so than Poker
with it's initial, random dealing of the cards.  No game is perfect
and no game should be perfect.  Games can only function as games when
driven by imperfect play.  Arguments based on theoretical perfect play
are stunningly irrelevant.
