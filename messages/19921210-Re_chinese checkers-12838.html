In article <1992Dec4.041753.20390@galileo.cc.rochester.edu> plsr@troi.cc.rochester.edu (Peter Lasersohn) writes:

In <1992Dec3.021335.21341@analog.com> gordon.cheung@analog.com writes:

On a move you can either move a piece one step to a neighboring opening,
or make a series of jumps. The source and destination of a jump must
be same distance from a single middle piece of any color. There cannot 
be any piece other then the middle piece along the jump path. Jumps can
be zig-zag'ed from one jump to the other.  [...]



Yow!  I'm certainly not used to playing with these long-distance jumps.
I (and the people I've played with) always assumed that you could only
jump an adjacent piece.  Anyone else out there play with long-distance
jumps?  I imagine it changes the game considerably.


Yep, I played it with long distance and zig-zag jumps. So you can do this
in one move, for example.

             F
            o
           .
            o
             .
              o
               .
              o
            .
           o
          S

Where S marks the starting position of your piece, F the final postion,
and 'o's represent your own or opponent pieces, and '.'s empty spots.
Definitely makes the game more interesing and fast.
