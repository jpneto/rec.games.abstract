|> For boards with odd M and N this could be analyzed as a Nim-like game
|> as described in Winning Ways by Conway.

Of course it could be (and should be). There isn't going to be an enormously
simple characterisation of the winning positions, because the subspace of
games played on 1xn rectangles is the octal game .07 (is this "Dawson's
Kayles"? I haven't got Winning Ways with me) in which a move takes exactly
two counters (points) from a pile (set of contiguous lattice points) and
then divides the pile into 2 piles, either or both of which may be empty.
This has Nim values that repeat with a period of 34 for n > 52 (the exceptional
n being 0, 1, 15, 17, 18, 32, 35, 52). In particular the winning positions for
the second player are n=0,1,15,35,34m+5,34m+9,34m+21,34m+25,34m+29 (m>=0).
