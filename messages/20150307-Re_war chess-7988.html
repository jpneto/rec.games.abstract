There is no need to be deliberately obscure. I go out of my way to try to not be obscure. I'm happy for people to know who I am and what I've done. To me, the purpose of a game is to be played and enjoyed. I make it easy to play my games. And I would be ecstatic to make some money, to help defray the costs of prototyping, playtesting, and traveling to show games at venues where they can get good playtesting. Every game of mine you find on the internet is very playable. While some may not like the style of games, I try to make my rules as clear and coherent as possible. I also listen to others' comments and critiques, and often enough incorporate suggestions. I see games as a somewhat cooperative venture between the designer and the players, and I am happy to get good ideas from people and use them where I may. 

I've run into a fair number of people who are afraid their ideas will be stolen, but it's been my experience that every designer has his or her own ideas, and is not interested in taking others' ideas. Ideas are cheap and easy, and a game design is not something actually worth stealing. A fully developed game might well be worth stealing, but by the time a design has been fully developed, there is almost always a "paper trail" - these days an electronic trail - that leads directly back to the designer of that game, and theft is generally plain. And copyright is every designer's right. 

To get to the specifics of Sozee, I find the rules could be written better. Your example for capture is obscure, and you really should have a section in the rules on what exactly the allowed geometric transforms are and how they work. You didn't, for instance, rotate 45 degrees, changing orthogonal to diagonal and vice versa, which is an available transform. 

Further, I have played an obscure published abstract at NonCon, held at Vassar College every year, that does use various geometric figures for scoring.. It's not identical to your game, but the two are clearly related. Your take on geometric scoring is the more obscure. (Grin, based on how you've presented yourself to me so far, you should *love* Stanley Random Chess!) It's been some years, and I cannot remember the name, but iirc it is something like or reminiscent of Shakespeare - but don't hold me strictly to that, as I have a terrible memory.

Finally, I'd like to ask you about playtesting; what did your playtesters say about Sozee? I get the impression that the game got little or no outside playtesting. For one thing, good playtesters would have ensured a better rules package. I encourage you to make more use of playtesters for your designs. They can be very helpful in turning a decent design idea into a good, well-developed game.
