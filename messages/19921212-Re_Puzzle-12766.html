In article <DBH.92Dec10095556@wombat.doc.ic.ac.uk> dbh@doc.ic.ac.uk (Denis Howe) writes:
|A man (with no parachute) drops a thousand feet onto a field and walks
|away unhurt.  How come?

Physics. This actually happens once in a while: I can remember seeing
three reports in the serious press in the last 20 years or so, two
involving parachutists and one a mountaineer (and I'm not a great reader
of news, by any means). The trick is that terminal velocity for a human
broad way on is something under 200km/h and fields aren't as hard as
those of us bred in the city are inclined to suppose. Flat is also the
right way to hit solid surfaces, I expect. It *is* unusual not to break
a bunch of bones in the process, but I'm sure it's possible; if *snow*
fields are allowed it's foolhardy but mechnically trivial.

But what's abstract about it? Or was this post your move, and your score
based on the responses? ;-)
