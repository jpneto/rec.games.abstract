Another digression:


Teaching reading by the whole word method, besides wasting years, seems to have
reduced or even destroyed the ability of a substantial proportion of children to
use the alphabet to pronounce words.


My siblings and I learned to read by "the whole word method" (also called "see and
say").  So did my younger son.  Possibly my other children as well, and I'm certain
it had a great influence on them.

The evidence (as explained to me:  I am not an expert on language acquisition) is
that "self-taught" readers overwhelmingly use the whole word method.  You just read
to the children a lot and suddenly they read.  Frankly, speaking works the same way
(in my experience).

However, it isn't really a method.  As you say, it is a terrible way to _teach_
reading.  Strangely, this is one of the few instances where pedagogical orthodoxy
followed research.  Studies showed that people who learned to read by the "whole word
method" were better readers than those who learned by "the phonics method".  So "the
educational establishment" jumped to the conclusion that the method was better.
However, as I indicated above, the real reason was that self-taught readers were
"whole word" readers and also (and I don't believe coincidentally) better readers.
Now that we have adults who were taught by the "whole word method" (what idiot came
up with that phrase, anyway?) we realize its shortcomings.
