Given a particular situation
in an area, it is fairy easy to determine the subset of pieces that
can influence the position over the next turn or two because you know
that there are no pieces with global influence. 


The maximum range of any piece (and that's only in the ortho-normal
directions) is 4 squares. If a piece is more than 4 squares away it
cannot come into contact with another piece in one move.

The push-pull rules also allow you to prune pieces which are providing
backup to the battle front from short-term analysis. They can't affect
your move on the opponent's next turn because they're blocked by their
own pieces.


The fact that you can
split your move among several pieces means that direct attacks are
fairly easy to foil, you have to play for a strategic position that
narrows your opponents options.


If you want to think about it in Chess terms, you can take up to 4
moves in each turn. If your opponent should maneuver a piece into a
pushing position (one where he could push on his next turn), you are
under no obligation to move out of the way. You might very well be
able to move multiple backup pieces into place. Pulls are rather more
complicated to defend; I don't really have any good ideas on that
topic, but it's fairly difficult to arrange enough clear space for
a pull to be useful.

So the big issue is maneuvering your pieces so that yous opponent is
encouraged and/or limited to playing the moves that you want from
him.
