It would be more accurate to say that Kasparov asserted that.  I don't

subsequent comments about the match that he still doesn't really

player, not a computer scientist).


The game I'm referring to did not occur in the final match that

correctness of the assertion.  Also, it was not just Kasparov's
assertion; others said the same thing.  I'm not aware of any
knowledgeable persons who made a contradictory claim about the

Your statement, "Computer programs are not supposed to be able to make
sacrifices for strategic reasons," is just wrong.


I'm not aware of even a single instance where a computer program
sacrificed a piece when it was not able to search the subsequent
position to a depth where it would regain the piece.  If you know
of any examples, I would welcome seeing them.  Otherwise, I stand
by my statement.



There's no reason at
all that computer programs can't use "strategic" as well as "tactical"
criteria, and in fact all reasonable computer chess programs, and
certainly Deep Blue, have lots of "strategic" evaluation criteria, not
just the raw evaluation of material.  It's certainly possible for such
programs to evaluate strategic (positional) factors as being worth more
than a material deficit (although this is of course more likely to apply
to a deficit of a pawn than of a whole piece).

                                        David desJardins


Of course computer program use other criteria besides just material
evaluation.  But I've never seen a position where a good computer
program correctly determined that strategic criteria outweighed
the loss of a piece.  The strategic evaluation of current computer
programs seems too primitive for this.  (They make up for a less
refined evaluation by infallible calculation and searching to a
depth beyond what any human can do.)
