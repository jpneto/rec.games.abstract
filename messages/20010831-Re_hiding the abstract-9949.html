 aside from the actual hidden information clause which technically
 rules games out from being in the "abstract" camp, consider this:


  The Seventh Seal, movie by Ingmar Bergman.

 Max Von Sydow, the knight, is playing a game of chess with Death.

 there is a moment between the long dran out game where he goes
 into an alehouse & confides to a stranger what his plans are in
 this game.  The stranger turns around to him & it is Death with
 a grin, saying "thanks.  i shall remember that."


 this is a dramatic illustrated of *permitted* hidden information.

 when you set a trap in an abstract game, you certainly dont tell
 your opponent what you are trying to do.

 the difference is this:  if the opponent is of sufficient strength,
 albeit close to superhuman for certain games, he should see your
 trap anyway & plan accordingly.  for a hidden information game,
 such as bridge, there is no sufficient strength possible to devine
 the hidden information.

 the whole idea of a trap is to assume that your opponent is not
 a perfect player at all & will make a mistake, infact certain
 *kinds* of mistakes that you can expect to a degree.
