In message <r1k7iizxy7.fsf@blue.ece.cmu.edu>, Jonathan R. Ferro <jferro@blue.ece.cmu.edu> writes
Nick Wedd <nick@maproom.co.uk> writes:
In all the abstract games which I enjoy, you can play so as to try to
improve your position.

In sprouts, either you are able to analyse the position fully (when
either you know that you have a won position, or you know that your
opponent has a won position but you can hope that he goes wrong), or you
can't and the move that you make might as well be random.

Isn't this statement true of all abstract games, not just sprouts?
(Note: this question is semi-rhetorical, in that I have my own reason
why it is not even true for sprouts.)

It is true of all those CGT (Combinatorial Game Theory) games, such as sprouts and nim, in which the values of all positions are stars.  In these games, the position is neutral between the players - all that matters is which player's turn it is, and whether an odd or an even number of moves will be made.  The players, of course, can influence the latter.

It is not true of chess, go, and most of the games discussed here (I see Zhadu, Octo, Arimaa, and Dvonn at present).  In these games, it is possible to make what appears to be a gain, without achieving a position that you can prove is a win.
