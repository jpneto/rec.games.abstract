When a game doesn't seem to be a metaphor or stylistic representation
of anything, it starts to seem like just a mathematical exercise to me.
That's when it starts to seem dry and boring--all work and no play.


Hence my attitude to "contrived" mechanisms in another posting.


It's kind of odd that I feel that way, though.  Checkers is more
compact and minimalistic than chess, so I ought to like it better for
those reasons.  But it's so starkly abstract that my imagination feels
left out every time I play.  I can't seem to connect the jump move (or
anything else about the game) with anything in real life, and that
bothers me.

In contrast, it's somehow easy for me to picture chess as a medieval
battle, despite the mobile castle towers and the superhero queen and
all the curious geometrical moves.  And because I can picture chess as
a stylized battle, I end up liking the game more.

But if we go just one more step toward "realism" and turn chess into a
more credible simulation of battle, suddenly it's not nearly as classy
anymore!  I was into wargames for many years, but I was never able to
think of any wargame as being in the same aesthetic league as chess.
The abstraction of chess makes it "bigger than life" in a sense,
whereas realistic wargames are merely literal representations of life.

I suppose an analogous pairing might be a Shakespearean play versus a
typical modern movie.  Shakespeare's work is somewhat abstract and his
characters "bigger than life," so his plays have a timeless quality to
them.  Modern movies, OTOH, are often topical--limited in time and
place so that their meaningfulness is less far-reaching.

A game like checkers (draughts) is perhaps more analogous to "modern
abstract art."  Mondriaan's work may be highly valued, but what does it
mean?  Since I can't mentally connect it with anything concrete, I'm
left wondering what statement (if any) it makes about life.  The
meaning of the work eludes me, and I have to rely on art critics to
interpret it for me.

So, I guess I don't like photographic realism, but I don't care for
pure abstraction either.  My taste runs to something in between.  And
among games, I'd say chess strikes that happy medium.
