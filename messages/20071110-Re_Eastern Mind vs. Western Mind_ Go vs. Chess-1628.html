What I'm getting around to is that a Chess-like game and a Go-like
game were designed by two different individuals, people found them
entertaining, and the games evolved, probably Chess more so than Go,
while retaining their essential identities.


It may be misleading to think of the origins of traditional board games
in terms of individuals. It is natural for us to do so because we live
in an age that has become extremely conscious of the significance and
importance of individuality. Folk games, like folk tales and folk
dances, are perhaps better regarded as the products of cultures and
communities rather than of individuals.


Chess and others have been evolutionary designs.  They start out and
the community needs to make adjustments to how things are.  Eventually
things settle down until enough people get unsettled with the way
things are and make changes.  With Chess, it was in India, and then
went east and west, and as it went west, the Muslims removed the dice
element, and then the crusaders increased mobility of pieces in order
to make the game play faster.  An interesting not here it is the West
that sees pieces with large mobility.

There are a LOT of questions in here as to why certain games catch on
in a certain area, and which shapes which.  An question in this line,
for example, would be why are pieces in the east more likely to lay
flat and not stand up in 3D.
