Agreed. In any case, the difference between Go and Chess is not that between east and west, since both are of Asiatic origin. Furthermore, Chess, in one form or another, remains pretty well the national game of Asia. While both are equally Asiatic, Wei-qi (the ancestor of Japanese Go) is more specifically Chinese, so the contrast is between China and the rest of Asia rather than between Asia and the west. Indeed, until about the middle of the 19th century "The West" has hardly come up with any original game of its own.

The observation that both Chess and Go come from Asia, if I may say so, misses the point. Chess, regardless of where it comes from, is more of a "force on force" game that is more commonly popular in the West, and in particularly in America, where such games as football and boxing have helped shape the popular imagination and given us metaphors for war and other forms of conflict.

In contrast, Go, a territorial game of maneuver, has had a hold on Asia that Xiangqi, Shogi and international Chess have never attained historically. Businessmen of Japan and military leaders of China often express their strategies in terms of Go whereas their American counterparts would never do so.

Is it a mere coincidence that many American businesses do not look beyond the next quarterly report and that many Asian business are renowned for developing business plans that stretch over years and decades?

So, yes, to answer your question, Chinese Chess is less Eastern than Go because it represents a "force on force" paradigm that has not been as influential as the territory/maneuver paradigm of Go in China. The former paradigm is predominant in the West in general, and in America in particular.
