This will probably not be of much help for the general case, but with
the modification that the board is of size Nx(N-1) and the second
player has to connect the shortest distance, the game is a sure win
for player two.



The strategy works as follows: divide the board into two equal-sized
parts along the short diagonal. Every time player one places a stone
in one half of the board, player two places a stone in the other half
at the position 180 degrees opposite (imagine rotating one board half
on top of the other). It is relatively easy to show that this will
eventually end in a win for player two.



I'm not sure if I understand you, but consider this sample game where
player two follows your strategy. N=5, long dimension lettered A-E,
short dimension numbered 1-4. Each cell is designated by a letter-number
pair. The corner cells are A1, E1, A4, E4. Let us say that E1 and A4 are
the obtuse corners, A1 and E4 the acute corners. Player 1's task is to
connect column A with column E. Player 2 must connect row 1 with row 4:



1.C2 C3 2.D2 B3 3.B2 D3 4.E2 A3 5.A2 and player 1 wins.


My mistake. My recollection was a bit vague: you shouldn't *rotate*
the other half of the board, you should *mirror* it across the
dividing line.

I have tried a few games with this strategy, and it does seem to work.
