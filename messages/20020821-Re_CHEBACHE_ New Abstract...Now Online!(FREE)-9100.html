Hi Carl,

Thanks for taking the time to help clear this up. Epecially, your
reference to the newsgroup guidelines.

In regards to the basic question of whether Chebache is an abstract
game or not. It seems to me that the term "abstract game" is not 
defined by whether it includes chance or not. There are simply 
abstract games with chance and those without. The newsgroup 
guideline that you reference...

"...discussion of abstract games where strategy, tactics and player 
skill are paramount."

... seems to basically say this as well by clarifying which group 
of abstract games that it is focusing on. Meaning that there is more 
than one category of abstract games. Chebache would clearly be
placed in a category including abstract games that include chance.
At least in regards to your reference. Do you agree?
 
You may feel that any game that includes chance is not worth your 
time and I respect that train of thought. I also love games of 
pure strategy, but, also enjoy playing strategy games that include 
an element of chance, because I feel that they are more reprentative 
of real life experience and can help one learn to prepare for the 
unknown variables, accidents or "freak" occurances in life and
may help people keep an open mind to things that they don't 
understand fully right off. Additionally, I think it can make 
for a more enjoyable game for a novice and beginner to play together, 
since it gives the beginner at least a sense of having a "chance" 
to win.

One of the thoughts that inspired me to design Chebache came from 
a general feeling that Backgammon was a bit to chance oriented for 
me and that most matches were essentially over half way through. 
This is partly due to the lack of defensive options. Once the 
player's pieces have passed each other along the path, it became 
simply a race off the board with no opportunity for interaction. 
So, without the inclusion of a doubling cube and the wagering process,
the game lacked interest and excitement from mid game on...at least 
for me. I also felt there were to many safes involved in that simply 
having 2 pieces together eliminated any risk to those pieces and their
positioning. In Chebache, there is rarely, if ever a situation or
position that cannot be threatened in some way. This makes it more 
challenging to way the benefits of stacking up for strength and 
holding a position or spreading out for more options and more fluid 
movement.

I've demonstrated how to play Chebache to hundreds, even thousands 
of people from age 5 and up and I assure you that most people have 
a pretty good understanding how to play after one game and that I 
can explain how to play in less than 5 minutes. Children seem to 
generally learn much faster, which I find fascinating. I think it's 
most likely due, at least in part, to them having a more open mind 
and stronger desire to try something new. Learning the strategies 
and how to blend them and keep them flexible are things that each 
player will develop at different rates and in different ways as in 
any strategy game. I firmly believe that it is an easier "in" than 
Chess and I think most would agree. This is assuming you are learning 
Chess for the first time. This is even more true if you were to learn 
Chess from a Rulebook. I think it is simply a matter of familiarizing 
yourself with the basic concepts of Chebache before trying to teach
it.

On another note, I do apologize for my "hyped up topic" in my post
and can understand your thoughts on this. I'm simply excited to 
finally have Chebache up and running Online and wanted to share it 
with others that enjoy trying new games...especially when it's a free 
offering. Thanks for mentioning that. I'll try to keep your thoughts 
in mind in the future. I wasn't thinking about the counter point to 
my use of CAPS, etc.

All the best,

Hello Everyone,

Chebache is a new abstract strategy board game which is now
available as an Online version with a FREE download at:
                     www.chebache.com

Chebache unites elements of Checkers, Backgammon and Chess and many
unique modes of play. It was designed to fill the gap between
Backgammon and Chess in regards to the balance of strategy and chance.
There are more options of movement and strategy than Backgammon, while
still including the chance elements of dice.
(Please read the excerpts from some of the reviews and comments about
Chebache at the end of this message.)

The Online version of Chebache, which is up and running as a trial
version is a FREE download, which will have occasional updates.
The latest version is 2.3.

*Note: Chebache Online will only run on PC (Windows) computers, for
now. Sorry to all Mac users, we hope to have a Mac version ready
sometime soon.

There are several tutorial aspects
included, which are explained in the "Help" section of the MENU, along
with the rules, notation and other features. Once online, please check
the message board for updates.

For the FREE download and general information about Chebache,
including reviews from On and Offline magazines and comments from
Chebache players, please take a moment to visit:

                      www.chebache.com

* The links for the download and the Chebache Egroups forum are in the
left margin of the home page, although we may move the link to a more
visible location soon.
