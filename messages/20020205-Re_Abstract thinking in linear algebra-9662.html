Well, there's algebra and there's algebra.  In high school (and at some
"college algebra" classes) algebra means solving polynomials (99.85% of
the time linear and quadratic) and doing some basic analytic geometry
(circles, parabolas, what not).


This is the least important part of algebra.

	 The really advanced of these algebra

classes get into small systems of equations (probably where you learned
to solve them).


Nobody should start with problems to be done with one
equation.  One does not need complicated methods to solve
arbitrarily large systems of linear equations, in any
case.  Successive elimination belongs at the beginning.

	 In college, "algebra" deals with, well,

"algebras"--sets and operations on them (groups, rings, fields, etc).
Linear algebra fits into this in a natural way, and matrices are the
"canonical" examples of linear operators in algebra.


A linear algebra course belongs after or within abstract
algebra.  Those who cannot understand abstract algebra will
only get the computer imitation part of linear algebra,
anyhow.
