Looking at the distance rule, I'm not convinced that Ayu is finite.

Say you're moving group A, and group B is closest, so you move that
way.  Then your opponent makes a move which alters your path lengths,
and now suddenly group A is closest to group C in the opposite
direction, so you have to reverse group A's course.   Etc. etc. etc.


Yes, but that sequence can't go on indefinitely, because your opponent
is already making progress in the meantime. A draw would require an
infinite series of intertwining, mutually path-blocking moves,
something I deem impossible.

I know it's very difficult to formally prove it, but it looks
intuitively clear enough to me, and I haven't been able to construct
any counterexamples, either, which is quite a lot to say.

I am positive that this is a finite mechanism, but if any not-
mathematically-challenged person out there can provide a formal proof,
I'll appreciate it. It would be an exciting read. Until then, you're
free to call it the "Ayu conjecture" ;-)
