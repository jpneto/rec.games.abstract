Typing "backgammon abstract strategy" into google primarily results in
links to the discussion here.  Not good.  The first real reference to
Backgammon specifically describes both Backgammon *and* Kriegspiel as
*not* being abstract strategy games.

"Abstract strategy games furthermore minimize the element of chance.
It is essential to their definition that such games have perfect
information: each player, when deciding his move, must have complete
information about the current position of the board (I include in
"position" qualities that may be physically undetectable, such as
whether a player may castle), or equivalently, about the original
position of the board and all moves made so far. Examples of perfect
information games would include Chess and Backgammon; games like
Stratego, Kriegspiel, or the recent Stealth Chess are not perfect
information games. Also, there must be no chance elements introduced
by such mechanisms as dice, cards, or dominos drawn at random:
Backgammon is not an abstract strategy game."


That is the Game Journal entry, which argues the combinatorial game
theory approach to abstract strategy games.


This definition would exclude Chess variants that have random elements
or hidden information.

Here's the link to Mark Thomson's well known, authoritative treatise
on abstract games:
