An idea I played with is not only to restrict the moves in the
build-up phase to additions of one or two pieces, buth also to
restrict the moves in the elimination phase to the removal of one or
two pieces (and - of course - in case of the elimination of two
pieces, to restrict these to adjacent pieces). 


The name "DoNIMo" seems appropriate for this game. :)

It actually makes an interesting game, not only in 2D, but even in 1D
(i.e. when restricting all polyominos to strips). In contrast to the
normal game of Nim, where a heap of N pieces has a trivial Grundy
value G(N) = N, in this game the Grundy values behave in a rather
erratic fashion:

 N  G(N)
 1   1
 2   2
 3   3
 4   1
 5   4
 6   3
 7   2
 8   1
 9   4
10   2

Is there any 'short-cut' for predicting G(N) for very large N? The
above suggests there isn't (apart from the obvious observation that no
single 1D-group can have a zero Grundy value).

In 2D, this game seems to have zero configurations for polyominos
starting from the tetrominos. These include the O-tetromino (the 2x2
square), and two pentominos (the T-pentomino and the F-pentomino).
