Subject: hexagonal tiling of the projective plane
From: Nick Wedd <nick@maproom.co.uk>
Date: 17/12/2008, 20:25
Newsgroups: rec.games.abstract

If you want to use a hexagonal grid on a projective plane for a game, you will find you can't.  There is no way to cover a projective plane entirely with hexagons.  Look up "Euler characteristic" if you want to know why.

However, you can come fairly close to a hexagonal grid, you just need to include a few smaller polygons.  The most regular ways use two triangles, or three squares, or six pentagons, and lots of hexagons.

Here is a way to build an arbitrarily large almost-hexagonal grid, with just five pentagons among the hexagons.  Start with a truncated icosahedron:  it has 12 pentagonal faces, 20 hexagonal, and 60 vertices. Replace each pentagon by a smaller pentagon, centred on the original and rotated through 36 degrees relative to it; replace each hexagon by a smaller hexagon, centred on the original and rotated through 30 degrees relative to it;  and replace each vertex by a hexagon.  Repeat the instructions of the previous sentence until you have enough hexagons. Right, now for the difficult bit:  we need to identify all the antipodal pairs of faces, antipodal pairs of pentagons, and antipodal pairs of vertices, ending up with a grid that has half as many of each.  A way to do this is to mark a set of edges that comes as close as possible to the "equator" of our near-spherical polyhedron, cut along it, and discard one half.  Now mark each pair of opposite hex-edges of the resulting near-hemisphere, to indicate that the hexes they belong to in fact border each other.  We have a projective plane, covered by a grid of many hexagons and six pentagons.  If we intend using it for a game, it will be sensible to project it onto a plane and print it on paper.

If instead of six equally-spaced pentagons we would rather have three evenly spaced squares, apply the same method, but start with a truncated octahedron instead of a truncated icosahedron.  Or for two triangles, start with a truncated tetrahedron.
