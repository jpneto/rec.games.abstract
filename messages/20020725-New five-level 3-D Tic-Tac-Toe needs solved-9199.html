I have created a new version of 3-D Tic-Tac-Toe that I believe does not have a first-move-win solution!  I need help with solving this game if it actually can be.  I would be willing to at least give you a free copy if you want to help.
 
The test marketing name of the game has been "Pentagonal" and it has been a success selling about 2000 in two years at about $30 (a bit much, but still worth it).  The patent for it has been bought (the construction mechanism has been patented, the game was invented in 1941) and is being tooled for mass production under another name which is not finalized yet.
 
This version is a significant improvement compared to the older versions of "Checkline", "Qubic" and others.  It seems (correct me if I wrong) that all previous versions have been a 4x4x4 matrix.  I have played this arrangement many times and found it to not be a sufficient challenge, especially against an 'average-thinker'.  Even if you know the secret (get an 'L' on any plane, and the game is over) and play against a real strategist, the 2nd player has to constantly follow the first player in order to make this a game.  The same goes for GO and other x-in-a-row strategy games.
 
By increasing the number of levels to five, the game takes on a completely new dimension.  Now the game an a center focus point, that becomes the key to the game.  The addition I have added to the game is to make this center space a 'free-space', much like bingo ended up doing.  This seemingly minor variation on the game may have created a close to mathmatical perfect game?  What I need is someone who knows how to use the proof-number search  or  dependency-based search  to determine if there is a first-player-to-move-force-win sequence.
 
I do not believe there is a first-move-win with this variation, since in effect both players are making the first move, and since neither can win outright without exclusive control of the center.  The game becomes a battle for the other critical strategy spaces, and a lot of the action is performed on the OUTSIDE spaces of the cube, instead of on the inside like a 4x4x4 version.
 
Plus I've designed it so you don't have to put anything together, and added a turntable for rotating the entire structure and get 360 degree viewing of the different angles.  The use of marbles is also a big improvement over the use of checkers, because they appear almost as molecules suspended in space.
