Don Green wrote:

My thinking at the time was that go achieves a lot of its computer
resistance through the sheer enormity of the board's dimensions; go on
a small grid is not necessarily resistant to computer analysis, just
as checkers on a 20x20 board is a lot more resistant to AI.  So I
tried to develop a resistant game without resorting to large boards or
large numbers of pieces.  Octi has just 7 pieces per side, which is a
fairly small number.  The 9x9 grid is spacious but doesn't overdo it.

I have confirmation that computers don't compete with humans on boards
as small as 9x9. Some people claim that 5x5 and 6x6 grids are solved but
provide neither mathematical proof nor source code. In fact, someone
fixed the supposed optimum sequence given on the site.

Of course there's the issue that computer go research does not benefit
from the same kind of financing as computer chess did.
