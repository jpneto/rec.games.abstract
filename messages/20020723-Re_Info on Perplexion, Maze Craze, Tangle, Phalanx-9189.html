Phalanx is played on an 8x8 board in which each square is further
divided by a diagonal into two isosceles triangles.  There are 16 2x2
blocks of squares which each have their four diagonals meeting at a
single corner. (I think that specifies the board...)


There's a one-page article on Phalanx in the current issue of *Abstract
Games* (#11, Autumn 2002).


Like 16 UK flags?


Rather.


The pieces are tiles (cardboard, in my set) the cover one, two or three
of these isosceles triangles.


Can you clarify the shape of these tiles? Is it:
1. isosceles triangle
2. square
3. square + attached triangle (2 shapes unless they can be flipped over).


They move in any orthogonal or diagonal direction, only over vacant
cells, and they cannot rest except in a position where their isosceles
triangle(s) are aligned with the ones on the board. They never rotate.


How do you ensure they don't rotate? Is there a directional arrow (other
marker?)?


By not rotating them. No.


If they don't rotate there must be two different versions of type 1. and
eight different versions of type 3.
How many of each?


Four of type 1. There are two of each in the starting position. Five of
type 2, square (4) and four orientations of a large isosceles triangle
(of which one is used). Twelve of type 3, of which two are used.

Rather than try ASCII art, I'll give the pieces in notation. in each of
the 64 squares there's a left triangle (a) and a right (b). Initial
setup is symmetrical horizontally and vertically. The lower set of
pieces is:
a1a a1b a2a (hoplite)
b1a (auxiliary)
b1b
b2a b2b (archer)
c1a
c2a c2b
d1a
e1b
d2b e2a (syntagma)
f1b
f2a f2b
g1a
g1b
g2a g2b
h1a h1b h2b

For more information, see the magazine.
