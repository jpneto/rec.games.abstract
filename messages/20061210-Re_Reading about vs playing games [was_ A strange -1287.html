The more I find out about backgammon (and I've been playing it for many,
many years) the more I realize how little I knew.  To those who don't play,
or who know little about the game, yes it's a racing game and yes, the
person who bears off all his or her checkers off first is the winner, but
there is MUCH room for strategy and maneuvering.  It's much more than just a
racing game, where the one who throws the higher numbers on the die is the
winner.  In fact, you don't always want to hit your opponent, (and send him
back) you don't always want to roll high numbers... (which may ruin your
timing).  Depending upon the initial rolls of the dice and how the game
develops, you can get into a running game, a priming game, a holding game, a
back game.  And I won't even get into the entire aspect about the
intricacies of the doubling cube, which is an skill and an art all by
itself.

As I said at the beginning of this post, there have been times when I
thought backgammon was "my game."  Periodically I'd get tired of
straining my brain over chess, and then I'd look for alternatives.
Backgammon was one alternative that stuck (another was cribbage).  It

to take the edge off competition (though I still felt all the

A downside to me is the doubling cube.  It's a great feature and adds
subtle strategic (and psychological) depth--but it also means that
quite often a game goes unfinished.  One player gives up, and nobody
ever knows what would have happened if the game had continued.  I'm not
sure, though, whether that bothers me more than the fact that *without*
the cube backgammon sometimes gets so lopsided that it's not worth
playing through.
